## Evidence Notes

### WMO Heat Health 2023

Use this source for the global heat early warning system coverage baseline. The 'fewer than 40 LMICs' figure is a binary existence indicator — it does not capture whether warnings reach vulnerable populations, trigger protective actions, or reduce mortality. Sub-national coverage variation is not captured. Do not use this as proof of effective heat protection in any specific LMIC without documentation of operational status, population reach, and response capacity.

### Ahmedabad Heat Action Plan

Use this source cautiously as the most-cited LMIC example. The 1,100 deaths per year estimate is based on all-cause mortality comparison, not heat-specific cause-of-death coding. The evaluation did not control for secular trends in healthcare, housing, and air conditioning. Ahmedabad is a large urban center with relatively good vital registration — the methodology may not transfer to rural or smaller-city LMIC contexts. Do not generalize from this single-city case study.

### Heat Mortality LMIC Data Gap

Use this source to document the fundamental evidence constraint: LMIC heat-mortality estimates are modeled from all-cause mortality with temperature exposure, not direct counts. Vital registration completeness varies enormously — some LMICs have less than 10 percent death registration. This means any heat early warning effectiveness evaluation in LMICs must use alternative data sources (sentinel surveillance, burial records) or all-cause mortality statistical methods with explicit data-quality caveats.

### Forecast-Based Financing for Heat

Use this source for operational context on forecast-triggered heat response, with the caveat that fewer than five countries have operational protocols. Fund release does not guarantee effective protective action. Temperature thresholds are based on historical climate statistics that may not account for changing heat-wave patterns. Do not generalize from the small number of operational cases.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
