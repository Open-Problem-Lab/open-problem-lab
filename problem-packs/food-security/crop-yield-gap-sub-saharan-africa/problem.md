# Crop Yield Gap Analysis For Smallholder Farmers In Sub-Saharan Africa

## Problem Statement

Sub-Saharan African cereal yields average 1.5 tons per hectare. With improved seed, fertilizer, and water management, 3+ tons is attainable — enough to double food production on existing farmland. The Global Yield Gap Atlas estimates the gap at country scale, but farmer-level yield constraints vary dramatically by soil type, rainfall, and market access. Sub-national yield gap analysis that connects satellite estimates to agronomic reality is the missing link.

> Which sub-national areas have the highest evidence-backed gap between actual and attainable crop yields, and which constraints are most tractable?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: FAO reports Sub-Saharan African cereal yields average 1.5 t/ha versus 5.6 in East Asia, with the gap attributed to soil degradation, limited fertilizer use, rainfed dependence, and weak extension services.
- Verified fact: The Global Yield Gap Atlas uses crop models and climate data to estimate water-limited yield potential at sub-national scale for major cereals across Africa.
- Verified fact: Satellite-derived yield estimates from MODIS and Sentinel-2 can detect within-field yield variation at 10-30m resolution, enabling farmer-scale gap analysis.

## Uncertain Areas

Whether satellite yield estimates are accurate enough for small fields under 0.5 ha, how soil constraints interact with climate variability to determine attainable yield, and whether yield gap maps change farmer practice or extension targeting.

## Initial Scope

Evidence inventory, satellite yield estimation, sub-national gap analysis, red-team review, field-reality review.

## Done Condition For V0

One reviewed and replicated sub-national crop yield gap analysis for at least two countries and two staple crops.
