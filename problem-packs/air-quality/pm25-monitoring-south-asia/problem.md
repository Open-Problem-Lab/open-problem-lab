# PM2.5 Monitoring Gaps And Health Impact In South Asia

## Problem Statement

South Asia is the global epicenter of ambient air pollution, with PM2.5 concentrations regularly exceeding WHO guidelines by an order of magnitude. The health burden — an estimated 2 million premature deaths annually across India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Nepal — is among the largest avoidable mortality risks on the planet. But the region's ground-level monitoring network is dangerously thin: India operates approximately 300 continuous ambient air quality monitoring stations for a population of 1.4 billion. Where there are no monitors, exposure must be estimated from satellites or models — and those estimates carry large uncertainty exactly where the burden is likely highest.

> Which PM2.5 monitoring gaps in South Asia create the largest health-burden uncertainty, and where could low-cost sensor networks or satellite-derived estimates fill the highest-priority gaps?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: WHO reports that 99 percent of the global population breathes air exceeding WHO guideline limits, with South Asia having the highest ambient PM2.5 concentrations globally.
- Verified fact: The Global Burden of Disease study estimates ambient PM2.5 caused approximately 2 million premature deaths in South Asia in 2019, with India alone accounting for over 1.6 million.
- Verified fact: India's Central Pollution Control Board operates approximately 300 continuous ambient air quality monitoring stations nationally — roughly one per 4.7 million people, concentrated in major cities.
- Verified fact: Satellite-derived PM2.5 estimates from MODIS, MISR, and SeaWiFS provide global coverage at approximately 0.01-degree resolution but have been validated primarily against monitors in well-monitored locations.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether satellite-derived PM2.5 estimates are accurate enough in unmonitored rural and peri-urban areas to substitute for absent ground monitors.
- How low-cost sensor networks perform against reference-grade monitors in the high-humidity, high-temperature South Asian environment.
- Whether identifying monitoring gaps leads to actual monitor deployment by national pollution control boards.
- How monitoring-gap prioritization interacts with existing regulatory monitoring mandates.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source quality assessment.
- Dataset availability including ground monitor data, satellite-derived PM2.5, and population data.
- Monitoring gap identification at sub-national scale.
- Priority ranking of gaps by population, baseline concentration, and uncertainty.
- Low-cost sensor network validation against reference monitors where co-located data exists.
- Red-team review of over-precise exposure estimates.
- Field-reality review with pollution control board context.

This pack does not accept sensor procurement recommendations, regulatory standard-setting, emissions source attribution, or unvalidated health impact forecasts.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated PM2.5 monitoring gap analysis for at least two South Asian countries, with explicit uncertainty communication and priority ranking methodology, that a domain reviewer judges suitable for monitoring-network planning discussion rather than deployment orders.
