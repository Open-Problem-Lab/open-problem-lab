## Evidence Notes

### McDonald et al. 2021 Source Apportionment Review

Use this source for the global source-attribution baseline with the critical caveat that LMIC megacity studies are sparse — Sub-Saharan African and Middle Eastern coverage is limited. The 20-60 percent household contribution range for South Asian cities is from a limited number of studies. Source contributions change over time with policy and economic transitions. Different apportionment methods give different results for the same city. Always document methodology choice and evidence gap.

### Source Apportionment Methodology Variability

Use this source to document that apportionment-methodology choice significantly affects attribution percentages — 10-30 percent of variance is explained by methodology alone. This means source-attribution claims must document which method was used and how results would differ under alternative methods. Receptor models require speciated PM2.5 data available at very few LMIC sites. Chemical transport models require emission inventories with higher uncertainty in LMIC cities with informal economic activity.

### Satellite PM2.5 LMIC Uncertainty

Use satellite PM2.5 estimates with the critical caveat that the satellite-to-ground transfer has 20-50 percent uncertainty in LMIC cities. Validation is limited by monitor sparsity. Satellite estimates may not capture near-source emission gradients. Seasonal variation in retrieval accuracy (cloud cover, dust) affects temporal representativeness. Always document uncertainty bounds and validation limitations.

### LMIC Megacity Monitor Sparsity

Use this source to document the fundamental evidence constraint: most LMIC megacities have fewer than 10 continuous PM2.5 stations for populations exceeding 10 million. This means city-wide source-attribution from a handful of sites has high spatial representativeness uncertainty. Monitor siting (curbside vs. urban background) affects source-attribution results. Document this sparsity explicitly in every output.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
