## Evidence Notes

### Ceam Seed Systems 2023

Use this source for the global adoption-gap baseline. The 'fewer than 10 percent' figure is an aggregate that masks wide variation by crop, country, and survey methodology. Seed-system data is fragmented across national surveys, CGIAR reports, and private-sector sources. Do not use this as proof of adoption rates in any specific crop-country combination without local survey data. Adoption rates may be outdated for specific contexts where recent seed-system investments have improved access.

### CGIAR Climate-Resilient Varieties

Use this source for understanding the supply side of the seed-system bottleneck — hundreds of climate-resilient varieties have been released, but delivery to farmers is the constraint. CGIAR documentation emphasizes releases, not adoption outcomes. Variety-release catalogs may not reflect current availability. Do not conflate variety releases with farmer access.

### TASAI Seed Access Index

Use TASAI for African seed-sector infrastructure assessment. The index measures national-level infrastructure, not farmer-level adoption — good TASAI scores do not guarantee farmer access at sub-national scale. TASAI covers Africa only; South Asian seed-system data requires different sources. Country assessments may reflect conditions from several years prior.

### On-Farm Yield Gap

Use this source to document the critical distinction between on-station trial performance and on-farm outcomes. The 30-50 percent yield gap means that adoption does not guarantee yield advantage under farmer conditions. The gap depends on complementary inputs (fertilizer, water, labor, extension) and may narrow as farmers gain experience. Always present yield-gap claims with crop-country specificity and methodology documentation.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
