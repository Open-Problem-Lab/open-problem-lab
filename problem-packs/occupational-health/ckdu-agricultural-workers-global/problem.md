# Chronic Kidney Disease Of Unknown Etiology In Agricultural Workers

## Problem Statement

Chronic kidney disease of unknown etiology (CKDu) is killing agricultural workers in their 30s and 40s across at least five world regions. It is not conventional CKD. It strikes young, lean laborers without diabetes, hypertension, or other standard risk factors. In El Salvador, CKD is the leading cause of hospital mortality. In Sri Lanka's North Central Province, community prevalence reaches 15 percent. In Nicaragua, sugar cane cutters show 10 to 14 percent prevalence. Similar clusters appear in Andhra Pradesh (India), the Nile Delta (Egypt), and Central Thailand.

The cause is fiercely contested. Two leading hypotheses dominate:

1. **Heat-stress nephropathy**: repeated episodes of heat exposure and dehydration during heavy manual labor in rising temperatures cause kidney injury through rhabdomyolysis, hyperuricemia, and chronic dehydration. This hypothesis predicts that hydration, shade, and work-rest scheduling would prevent new cases.

2. **Agrochemical exposure**: pesticides (particularly glyphosate and paraquat), heavy metals (arsenic, cadmium, lead), or contaminated water cause renal injury through toxic or inflammatory pathways. This hypothesis predicts that pesticide regulation and water remediation would prevent new cases.

These two hypotheses imply entirely different regulatory actions, different responsible parties, and different funding streams. A verified evidence synthesis that can distinguish between them, or that can establish which dominates in which context, would redirect occupational health regulation in affected countries and research funding at NIH, WHO, and the International Society of Nephrology.

The v0 problem is not "solve CKDu." That is too broad and too medically uncertain. The sharper problem is:

> Which causal hypothesis for CKDu has the strongest reproducible evidence, and would a verified assessment change how occupational health ministries and research funders prioritize heat-stress regulation versus pesticide regulation?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: CKDu prevalence among agricultural workers in Mesoamerica ranges from 10 to 14 percent in screened cohorts, with the highest rates among sugar cane cutters in the hottest lowland regions (Wesseling et al., 2020).
- Verified fact: In Sri Lanka, CKDu prevalence in the North Central Province farming communities is approximately 15 percent, with a male predominance and onset in the fourth decade of life (Jayatilake et al., 2015).
- Verified fact: El Salvador has the highest CKD mortality rate in the Americas, with CKD as the leading cause of hospital deaths (Pan American Health Organization, 2020).
- Verified fact: The WHO, International Society of Nephrology, and Lancet have all called CKDu a public health emergency requiring urgent investigation.
- Verified fact: CKDu is not tracked as a distinct category in the Global Burden of Disease study; it is aggregated under all-cause CKD, making burden estimation and cross-country comparison difficult.

## Uncertain Areas

- The relative contribution of heat-stress versus agrochemical exposure to CKDu incidence. Most studies are cross-sectional and cannot establish causation.
- Whether the disease in different regions (Mesoamerica, Sri Lanka, India, Egypt, Thailand) shares a single mechanism or represents different diseases with similar presentation.
- The role of infectious diseases (leptospirosis, hantavirus) as co-factors, particularly in Mesoamerica.
- Whether safe water access and sanitation conditions confound the agrochemical hypothesis.
- The true global scale: no surveillance system reports CKDu separately, so the 5 to 10 million global estimate is uncertain.
- Whether acclimatization and individual susceptibility (genetic, metabolic) modify the heat-stress response.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory of prevalence studies, causal hypothesis studies, and surveillance data across affected regions.
- Dataset compilation of reported CKDu prevalence by country, region, study population, and diagnostic criteria.
- Causal evidence synthesis separating heat-stress, agrochemical, and infectious-disease hypotheses by evidence strength and transferability.
- Intervention evidence review (hydration programs, work-rest scheduling, pesticide regulation, water filtration) with failure modes and field constraints.
- Red-team review of how causal claims could be misused to deflect regulatory responsibility or shift blame to workers.
- Field-reality review from occupational health ministries, nephrology associations, and agricultural worker organizations.

This pack does not accept clinical treatment protocols, individual worker health determinations, or pesticide liability claims.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated evidence synthesis that ranks the causal hypotheses by evidence strength and names the specific regulatory or funding decision that the ranking would change, with clear limits on what the evidence can and cannot prove.
