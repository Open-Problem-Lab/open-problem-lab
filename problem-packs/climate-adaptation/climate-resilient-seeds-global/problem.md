# Climate-Resilient Seed Access And Adoption Gaps In Smallholder Agriculture

## Problem Statement

Fewer than 10 percent of smallholder farmers in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia use improved, climate-resilient seed varieties — drought-tolerant maize, flood-tolerant rice, heat-tolerant wheat, and early-maturing legumes — despite decades of breeding-program investment by CGIAR centers and national agricultural research systems. Climate change is shifting the agro-ecological zones for which existing varieties were bred: maize zones in East Africa are warming faster than breeding programs have produced adapted varieties, and rainfall variability is making single-trait drought tolerance insufficient.

The seed-system bottleneck is not primarily genetic: climate-resilient varieties exist in breeder collections and national variety-release catalogs. The bottleneck is delivery — seed multiplication, distribution, and last-mile retail to smallholders — combined with farmer awareness, affordability, and trust in new varieties. Sub-national data on where climate-resilient varieties are available for sale, what share of farmers plant them, and what yield advantage they provide under actual farmer conditions (versus on-station trials) is almost entirely absent from public datasets.

> Which sub-national agricultural areas in SSA and South Asia have the largest evidence-backed gap between climate-resilient seed availability and actual adoption, and which seed-system barriers are most tractable?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: A 2023 systematic review by Ceam et al. found that fewer than 10 percent of smallholder farmers in SSA and South Asia use improved climate-resilient seed varieties, with access constrained by seed-multiplication capacity, distribution-network reach, and farmer awareness.
- Verified fact: CGIAR centers (CIMMYT, IRRI, ICRISAT, CIAT) and national agricultural research systems have released hundreds of climate-resilient varieties over the past two decades, but seed-system bottlenecks mean that varieties reach a fraction of their target farmers.
- Verified fact: The African Seed Access Index (TASAI) tracks seed-sector development indicators — variety release, seed production, quality control — across 20+ African countries, providing the most systematic cross-country seed-system data.
- Verified fact: The difference between on-station yield trials (where new varieties are typically tested under optimal management) and on-farm yields (where farmers face input, labor, and knowledge constraints) can be 30-50 percent, meaning that adoption is not just about seed access but about the complementary inputs and extension that make new varieties perform.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether TASAI and similar seed-system indicators predict actual farmer-level adoption at sub-national scale.
- How farmer preferences — for taste, cooking quality, storage characteristics, and marketability — interact with climate-resilience traits in adoption decisions.
- Whether seed-system gap maps change government seed-subsidy policy, private-sector distribution investment, or CGIAR breeding-priority decisions.

## Initial Scope

Evidence inventory, seed-system data compilation, adoption-gap mapping, barrier classification, red-team review, field-reality review.
