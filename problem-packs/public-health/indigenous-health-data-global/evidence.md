## Evidence Notes

### UN Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues: State of the World's Indigenous Peoples (Health)

Use this source for the foundational documentation of indigenous health disparities — life-expectancy gaps of 5-20 years between indigenous and non-indigenous populations across all countries with data (Australia: approximately 8 years; Canada: 10-15 years; New Zealand: approximately 7 years; Latin American countries: variable). The report also documents that fewer than 30 countries include indigenous identifiers in routine health data collection, and fewer than 10 operate indigenous-governed health data systems.

Indigenous population data depends on self-identification and census methodology, and no single definition of indigeneity applies across all countries. Do not use these cross-country estimates as comparable metrics — they reflect different measurement instruments, population definitions, and health-system contexts. The UNPFII report is primarily a documentation of data absence, not a systematic estimate of the gap magnitude.

### CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance Context

The CARE Principles — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, and Ethics — were developed by the Global Indigenous Data Alliance (GIDA) and have been formally endorsed by the UN Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues, the Wellcome Trust, and several national research-funding bodies. The principles establish that indigenous peoples must govern the collection, ownership, and application of data about their communities. Any health-access gap analysis involving indigenous data must document CARE Principles compliance for every data source, including consent, governance, and benefit-sharing arrangements.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
