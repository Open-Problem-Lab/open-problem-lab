# Cold Spell Mortality Risk And Heating Access Gaps In Low-Income Countries

## Problem Statement

Cold temperatures kill substantially more people globally than heat — a 2015 Lancet study by Gasparrini et al. analyzing 74 million deaths across 13 countries found that cold-attributable mortality exceeded heat-attributable mortality by a factor of approximately 20 to 1. The mortality burden is concentrated in populations without adequate heating, insulation, and cold-weather protection — precisely the populations that are least studied. Cold-spell mortality research is overwhelmingly concentrated in high-income temperate countries, and cold-spell mortality data from LMICs — including high-altitude regions in South Asia, East Africa, and the Andes where cold exposure is common — is almost entirely absent.

The 2015 Lancet study analyzed data from 384 locations in 13 countries, of which only one (Thailand) was a lower-middle-income country and the remainder were high-income. Extrapolating cold-mortality risk from London or New York to Kathmandu, Addis Ababa, or La Paz is epidemiologically invalid without local data. The absence of basic cold-mortality surveillance in most LMICs means that the true cold-attributable mortality burden — and the heating-access gap that underlies it — remains unmeasured.

> Which sub-national areas in LMICs are likely to have the highest cold-attributable mortality risk based on temperature, housing quality, and heating-access data, and where are the evidence gaps most severe?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: Gasparrini et al. (2015, The Lancet) analyzed 74 million deaths in 384 locations across 13 countries and found that cold temperatures caused substantially more deaths than heat, with cold-attributable mortality concentrated in populations without adequate heating and insulation. Only one LMIC (Thailand) was included in the analysis.
- Verified fact: Sub-national cold-spell mortality data is almost universally absent from LMIC vital registration systems, which already undercount total mortality in most low-income countries. Cold-attributable deaths are rarely coded as such even in high-income settings.
- Verified fact: Housing-quality surveys (DHS housing modules, census data on construction materials) provide indicators of heating access and thermal insulation — such as wall material, roof material, and heating-fuel type — that can serve as proxies for cold-exposure vulnerability in the absence of direct cold-mortality data.
- Verified fact: ERA5-Land temperature reanalysis at 0.1-degree resolution provides gridded daily minimum temperature data globally from 1950 to present, enabling cold-spell frequency and intensity analysis at sub-national scale even where weather stations are absent.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether the temperature-mortality relationship estimated from high-income temperate countries can be cautiously extrapolated to LMIC populations with similar temperature exposures but different housing, nutrition, and health-system contexts.
- How heating-access indicators from DHS housing modules correlate with actual indoor temperatures during cold spells — a household with a metal roof and no insulation may have indoor temperatures nearly identical to outdoor temperatures.
- Whether passive-solar design, improved cookstoves that provide space heating, and insulation programs could reduce cold-attributable mortality at costs affordable in LMIC contexts.
- How cold-spell mortality risk interacts with altitude — millions of people live above 2,000 meters in the Andes, East African highlands, and Himalayan foothills where nighttime temperatures routinely drop below freezing for part of the year.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for cold-spell temperature data, temperature-mortality studies, housing-quality survey data, and heating-access indicators.
- Sub-national cold-spell frequency and intensity mapping from ERA5-Land temperature reanalysis.
- Heating-access vulnerability mapping from DHS and census housing-quality data.
- Cold-spell mortality risk estimation with explicit documentation of extrapolation assumptions and data-absence caveats.
- Red-team review of mortality-risk overstatement and the risk of cold-risk communication being dismissed as less urgent than heat.
- Field-reality review with housing, energy, and health authorities in high-altitude and cold-exposed LMIC regions.

This pack does not accept heating-fuel subsidy recommendations, building-code proposals, or unvalidated cold-mortality forecasts for specific populations.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national analysis of cold-spell mortality risk and heating-access gaps for at least two LMIC countries or regions, with explicit documentation of temperature-mortality extrapolation assumptions, temperature-data validation, and housing-quality proxy limitations.
