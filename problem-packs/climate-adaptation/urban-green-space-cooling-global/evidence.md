## Evidence Notes

### Bacci et al. 2022 Meta-Analysis

Use this source for the cooling-effect baseline with the critical caveat that nearly all included studies are from high-income temperate cities. The 1-5°C cooling range and 2-5 percent mortality-reduction estimate have not been validated in LMIC tropical and subtropical urban contexts. The meta-analysis does not distinguish irrigated from unirrigated green space — in water-scarce LMIC cities, this distinction is critical. Do not apply pooled cooling estimates to specific LMIC neighborhoods without local calibration.

### Landsat LST Urban Heat Island

Use Landsat LST for intra-urban heat-island mapping with the critical caveat that LST measures surface radiative temperature, not the air temperature that drives heat-stress physiology. The LST-to-air-temperature relationship varies by climate zone, humidity, and urban geometry — it has not been calibrated for most tropical LMIC cities. The 16-day revisit cycle with cloud gaps may miss peak heat-wave conditions. Always document LST-to-air-temperature transfer limitations.

### Informal Settlement Green-Space Deficit

Use this source to document the environmental injustice dimension — informal settlements have 5-10x less green space and 3-8°C higher surface temperatures. Green-space measurement in informal settlements is complicated by mixed land use and vegetation that satellites may miss. The studies are cross-sectional — they do not establish that adding green space in informal settlements would produce the predicted cooling effect.

### Irrigation-Cooling Dependence

Use this source to document the fundamental constraint for green-space cooling in water-scarce LMIC cities. Unirrigated vegetation provides minimal cooling during heat waves when cooling is most needed. This finding may not apply to deep-rooted native species, but it creates a critical planning constraint. Irrigation requirements may conflict with drinking-water and agricultural demand.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
