# Dust Storm PM10 Exposure And Respiratory Health Impact In Arid And Semi-Arid Regions

## Problem Statement

Dust storms are an increasing health hazard across arid and semi-arid regions — the Middle East, North Africa, Central Asia, the Sahel, and parts of South Asia — driven by land degradation, drought, and changing wind patterns. Mineral dust contributes up to 50 percent of PM10 in arid regions during storm events and carries biological and chemical contaminants including bacteria, fungi, endotoxins, heavy metals, and pesticide residues. Respiratory hospitalizations increase 5-10 percent during dust events in studied populations, with cardiovascular and neonatal effects also documented but less systematically studied.

Satellite aerosol optical depth products from MODIS and CALIOP can detect dust plumes at 1-10 km resolution, and the Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service provides dust-forecast models. But linking satellite-detected dust exposure to verified respiratory health outcomes at sub-national scale in LMIC arid regions — where ground air-quality monitoring is sparse and vital registration is incomplete — remains underdeveloped. The confounding of dust-specific PM10 with co-occurring sources (construction, traffic, biomass burning) complicates health-impact attribution.

> Which arid and semi-arid LMIC regions have the strongest evidence-backed relationship between dust-storm PM10 exposure and respiratory health outcomes, after accounting for non-dust PM10 confounders?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: A 2014 review by Goudie found that dust storms contribute up to 50 percent of PM10 in arid regions during storm events and are associated with 5-10 percent increases in respiratory hospitalizations, with effects strongest in children, elderly, and pre-existing respiratory conditions.
- Verified fact: MODIS aerosol optical depth and CALIOP lidar data detect dust plumes at 1-10 km resolution, with the Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service providing dust-forecast models at 40 km resolution globally.
- Verified fact: Mineral dust carries biological and chemical contaminants — bacteria, fungi, endotoxins, heavy metals, and pesticide residues — that may contribute to health effects beyond the mineral particle mass itself, making dust PM10 potentially more harmful per unit mass than non-dust PM10.
- Verified fact: Ground air-quality monitoring in LMIC arid regions is extremely sparse — most Sahelian, Central Asian, and Middle Eastern LMIC countries have fewer than one PM10 monitor per million people, making satellite-to-ground exposure validation difficult.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether satellite AOD adequately proxies for ground-level dust PM10 during storm events, particularly in bright-surface deserts where AOD retrieval accuracy is reduced.
- The extent to which dust-specific health effects are driven by mineral particle mass versus the biological and chemical contaminants dust carries — the toxicological profile varies by dust source region.
- Whether dust-health maps change land-degradation mitigation, dust-early-warning, or health-system preparedness decisions in arid LMIC regions.
- How dust-storm frequency and intensity are changing under climate change and land-use pressure — trends vary by region and are not uniformly increasing.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for satellite dust-detection products, ground PM10 monitors, and respiratory health-utilization data.
- Sub-national dust-exposure mapping from satellite AOD and dust-forecast models, validated against available ground monitors.
- Health-impact analysis linking dust-specific PM10 exposure to respiratory outcomes with appropriate confound controls for non-dust sources.
- Red-team review of exposure-misclassification and health-impact overstatement risks.
- Field-reality review with air-quality management agencies and health ministries in arid LMIC regions.

This pack does not accept land-degradation mitigation recommendations, dust-suppression technology proposals, or unvalidated health-impact forecasts for specific dust events.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national dust-exposure health-impact analysis for at least two LMIC arid regions, with explicit documentation of satellite-to-ground exposure validation, dust-source toxicology context, and non-dust PM10 confound controls.
