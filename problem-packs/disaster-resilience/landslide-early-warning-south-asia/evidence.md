## Evidence Notes

### Petley et al. 2024 Global Landslide Fatalities

Use this source for the global landslide fatality baseline. The 50 percent figure for South Asia is an aggregate — individual country and district rates vary substantially. Fatality data undercounts events in remote Himalayan valleys. The database combines rainfall-triggered and earthquake-triggered landslides — disentangling triggers requires event-specific analysis. Always document these limitations in susceptibility analyses.

### InSAR Slope Deformation Monitoring

Use Sentinel-1 InSAR for ground-deformation detection with the critical caveat that performance is limited in steep, forested Himalayan terrain. InSAR coherence is lost in vegetated areas. Steep slopes create geometric distortions. Atmospheric water vapor introduces phase delays. The 6-12 day revisit cycle limits warning lead time. Always document these terrain-specific limitations.

### NASA Global Landslide Hazard Assessment

Use NASA's susceptibility model with the critical caveat that 1km resolution smooths micro-topographic variations. Rainfall inputs have reduced accuracy in mountainous terrain. Validation in Himalayan countries is limited. Susceptibility maps are probabilistic, not predictive — they estimate relative likelihood, not absolute probability or timing. Always document the susceptibility-vs-prediction distinction.

### Nepal Landslide Early Warning Pilot

Use this source for operational context. The pilot covers only a few districts — national coverage is years away. Warning-to-evacuation effectiveness has not been tested against a major event. Rainfall thresholds are based on regional data that may not capture micro-climate variation. Sustainability depends on continued funding. Always present as a pilot with limited coverage, not as an operational system.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
