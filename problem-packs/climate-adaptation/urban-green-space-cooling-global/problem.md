# Urban Green Space Cooling Effect And Heat Mortality Reduction Potential

## Problem Statement

Urban green spaces — parks, street trees, green roofs, and vegetated open areas — reduce local ambient temperature by 1-5°C through shading and evapotranspiration, with the cooling effect extending 100-500 meters into surrounding neighborhoods depending on green-space size, vegetation type, and urban morphology. A 2022 meta-analysis estimated that a 10 percent increase in urban green space could reduce heat-attributable mortality by 2-5 percent. But nearly all studies in that meta-analysis came from high-income cities in temperate climates. The cooling effectiveness of green space in the compact, densely built, rapidly growing cities of South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and the Middle East — where heat mortality risk is highest — is largely unmeasured.

Satellite-derived land-surface temperature from Landsat at 30-100m resolution and vegetation indices (NDVI) enable intra-urban heat-island mapping and green-space cooling quantification. Global urban-boundary datasets and WorldPop population data exist. But linking green-space distribution to neighborhood-level heat exposure and population vulnerability — particularly for informal settlements where both green space and temperature monitoring are absent — has not been done systematically for LMIC cities.

> Which neighborhoods in LMIC cities have the strongest evidence-backed gap between green-space cooling capacity and heat-exposure vulnerability?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: A 2022 meta-analysis by Bacci et al. found urban green spaces reduce ambient temperature by 1-5°C with cooling extending 100-500 meters, and estimated 2-5 percent heat-mortality reduction per 10 percent green-space increase.
- Verified fact: Landsat thermal-infrared data at 30-100m resolution provides land-surface temperature for intra-urban heat-island mapping since 1984. NDVI from the same sensors quantifies vegetation density as a green-space proxy.
- Verified fact: Informal settlements in LMIC cities typically have 5-10 times less green space per capita than formal residential areas and surface temperatures 3-8°C higher due to construction materials, building density, and lack of vegetation.
- Verified fact: The cooling effect of urban green space depends on irrigation — in water-scarce cities, unirrigated vegetation provides minimal cooling during heat waves when cooling is most needed.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether satellite land-surface temperature (which measures surface radiative temperature) adequately proxies for the air temperature that drives heat-stress physiology, particularly in humid climates.
- How the cooling-benefit-per-unit-area of different green-space types (parks, street trees, green roofs, community gardens) varies by climate zone and urban density.
- Whether green-space cooling maps change municipal land-use planning, tree-planting programs, or informal-settlement upgrading decisions.

## Initial Scope

Evidence inventory, satellite green-space and LST mapping, cooling-effect estimation, vulnerability-weighted heat-risk mapping, red-team review, field-reality review.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated neighborhood-scale green-space cooling and heat-vulnerability analysis for at least two LMIC cities, with explicit satellite-to-ground temperature validation and green-space-type differentiation.
