# Agricultural Residue Burning Air Quality Impact In South Asia

## Problem Statement

Every October and November, farmers across Punjab, Haryana, and Uttar Pradesh burn an estimated 30 million tons of rice paddy residue to clear fields for wheat planting — creating a seasonal air-pollution crisis that blankets Delhi and the Indo-Gangetic Plain in smoke. MODIS satellite fire detection records over 80,000 individual fires during the peak burning weeks. Agricultural residue burning contributes an estimated 20-30 percent of peak-season PM2.5 in Delhi and 10-15 percent across the broader Indo-Gangetic Plain, compounding industrial, vehicular, and household pollution sources.

NASA's MODIS and VIIRS satellite instruments detect active fires at 375m-1km resolution, and the Global Fire Emissions Database provides emissions estimates tied to burned-area maps. But linking satellite-detected fires to verified ground-level PM2.5 concentrations from the sparse Central Pollution Control Board monitoring network — and attributing the health impact specifically to agricultural burning versus other co-occurring sources — requires source-apportionment modeling that is not systematically validated at sub-national scale.

> Which sub-national areas in South Asia have the strongest evidence-backed relationship between satellite-detected agricultural residue burning and respiratory health outcomes, after accounting for non-burning PM2.5 sources?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: A 2022 systematic review by Jack et al. estimated that agricultural residue burning contributes 20-30 percent of peak-season PM2.5 in Delhi during October-November and 10-15 percent across the broader Indo-Gangetic Plain during post-harvest periods, based on source-apportionment studies using chemical tracer analysis and satellite fire-detection data.
- Verified fact: MODIS (Terra and Aqua) and VIIRS satellite instruments detect active agricultural fires at 375m-1km resolution, with the NASA Fire Information for Resource Management System providing near-real-time fire-alert data. The Global Fire Emissions Database (GFED) provides emissions estimates including black carbon, organic carbon, and PM2.5 from agricultural burning.
- Verified fact: India's CPCB operates approximately 300 continuous ambient air-quality monitoring stations nationally — roughly one per 4.7 million people, heavily concentrated in urban areas and sparse in the rural Punjab-Haryana agricultural belt where burning occurs.
- Verified fact: The Indian government's crop-residue management program has provided over 200,000 subsidized Happy Seeder machines and other no-burn equipment to farmers, but burning continues at large scale — approximately 50-70 percent of rice residue is still burned in Punjab despite eight years of the program.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether MODIS and VIIRS fire-detection products miss small fires and smoldering burns that produce substantial PM2.5, leading to systematic underestimation of burning emissions.
- The extent to which source-apportionment models can reliably separate agricultural-burning PM2.5 from residential biomass burning, brick kilns, and diesel generators during the same season, given overlapping chemical signatures.
- Whether satellite-derived PM2.5 exposure from agricultural burning correlates with health-outcome data (respiratory hospital admissions, mortality) at district scale in the absence of dense ground monitoring.
- How the economics of alternative residue-management practices — particularly the cost of Happy Seeder rental and the labor-time window between rice harvest and wheat planting — interact with burning decisions in ways that a technical intervention model alone cannot capture.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for satellite fire-detection products, ground PM2.5 monitors, source-apportionment models, and respiratory health-utilization data.
- Sub-national mapping of agricultural-burning fire activity from MODIS and VIIRS data, validated against available ground-monitor PM2.5 data during burning and non-burning periods.
- Source-specific health-impact analysis linking burning-attributed PM2.5 to respiratory outcomes with careful confound control for co-occurring pollution sources.
- Red-team review of source-attribution overstatement and the risk that burning-focused interventions ignore other PM2.5 sources.
- Field-reality review with agricultural extension services, farmer organizations, and state pollution control boards.

This pack does not accept crop-residue management technology recommendations, farmer-subsidy proposals, or unvalidated burning-to-health attribution chains.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national analysis linking satellite-detected agricultural residue burning to PM2.5 concentrations and respiratory health outcomes for at least two states in the Indo-Gangetic Plain, with explicit documentation of source-apportionment methodology, ground-monitor validation status, and non-burning PM2.5 confound controls.
