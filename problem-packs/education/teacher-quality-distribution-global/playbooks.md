# Playbooks

## Analyst Playbook

1. Start with `problem.json`, `evidence.json`, and `tasks.json`.
2. Select one scoped task.
3. Add evidence before adding claims.
4. For teacher-distribution mapping: always document EMIS data quality, update frequency, and qualification-definition comparability across districts and countries.
5. For deployment-equity analysis: always document that teacher qualification is a proxy for teaching effectiveness — the proxy-to-effectiveness relationship is weak in many contexts, and SDI surveys show that even qualified teachers may score poorly on content-knowledge assessments.
6. For absence-informed analysis: always document that absence rates are measured through unannounced visits at a point in time and that rural absence may reflect systemic factors (long commutes, poor infrastructure) not individual teacher failure.
7. Mark every operational inference as reviewed, replicated, or blocked.

## Reviewer Playbook

Ask five questions:

1. What claim is being made about teacher-qualification distribution or deployment equity?
2. What source proves it — and is it EMIS data, an SDI survey, or a learning-outcome assessment?
3. What method translates qualification distribution into deployment-equity claims, and what is the qualification-to-effectiveness proxy validity?
4. What would make it false — particularly if EMIS data is outdated, if qualification definitions don't capture teaching quality, or if absence data reflects systemic factors rather than individual failure?
5. Who could misuse it — particularly to justify punitive teacher transfers, to stigmatize rural teachers, or to treat qualification as equivalent to effectiveness?

## Field-Reality Playbook

Do not ask whether a deployment-equity map is impressive. Ask whether it would change a named decision for a named user at a useful time, without creating worse incentives. For teacher deployment in low-income countries, ask specifically:

- Would a Kenya Ministry of Education teacher-deployment director use this to target rural hardship allowances, or would it be used for punitive transfers that worsen rural retention?
- Would a teacher-training institution use this to target outreach to underserved districts, or would it treat qualification as equivalent to effectiveness when the evidence shows the link is weak?
- Would a rural-incentive program designer use this to calibrate incentive levels, or would absence data be used to stigmatize rural teachers without addressing the systemic factors driving absence?

The answer must include explicit non-use cases before any field-facing output is published.
