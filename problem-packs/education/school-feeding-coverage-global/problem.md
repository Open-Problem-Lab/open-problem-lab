# School Feeding Program Coverage And Nutritional Impact Gaps In Low-Income Countries

## Problem Statement

WFP estimates 418 million children receive school meals globally, making school feeding the most widespread social safety net in many countries. But coverage in low-income countries is below 20 percent — the children who most need school meals are least likely to receive them. School feeding programs serve dual purposes: improving nutrition and increasing enrollment and attendance. Yet the evidence base for nutritional impact is weaker than the evidence for enrollment effects, and coverage data rarely distinguishes programs by meal quality, nutritional content, or targeting effectiveness.

School feeding coverage data comes from WFP's annual survey and national program reports, but sub-national coverage variation is rarely documented. Many countries report national coverage statistics that mask enormous district-level gaps — urban schools may have near-universal coverage while rural schools have none. The gap between national program existence and verified sub-national coverage is the core measurement problem.

> Which sub-national districts in low-income countries have the strongest evidence-backed gap between school feeding program existence and verified coverage, enrollment, and nutritional impact?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: WFP's 2024 State of School Feeding report estimates 418 million children receive school meals globally, with low-income country coverage below 20 percent and enormous variation across regions.
- Verified fact: School feeding programs increase enrollment by 8-12 percentage points and attendance by 5-9 percentage points on average in low-income countries, but nutritional-impact evidence is weaker — meal quality, micronutrient content, and dietary diversity vary enormously across programs.
- Verified fact: Coverage data is typically reported at national level — sub-national coverage variation within countries is rarely documented, meaning national statistics mask district-level gaps where rural and remote schools may have no feeding programs.
- Verified fact: The cost-effectiveness of school feeding relative to alternative nutrition interventions (supplementary feeding, cash transfers, micronutrient supplementation) is debated — school feeding costs $0.10-$0.50 per child per day depending on program design and local food prices.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether sub-national coverage data exists at sufficient detail for targeting analysis, or whether only national aggregate statistics are available in most countries.
- How meal quality and nutritional content vary across programs — a coverage metric that does not capture meal adequacy may overstate nutritional impact.
- Whether school feeding coverage maps change program expansion targeting, meal-quality standards, or alternative nutrition-investment decisions.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for WFP program data, national school feeding reports, enrollment and attendance data, and nutrition-outcome surveys.
- Sub-national coverage mapping using WFP survey data and national program documentation.
- Nutritional-impact analysis linking coverage to anthropometric and dietary-diversity outcomes where data exists.
- Red-team review of coverage overstatement and the risk that national statistics mask district-level gaps.
- Field-reality review with education ministries and school feeding program managers.

This pack does not accept school feeding program design recommendations, meal-quality standards, or unvalidated claims that specific programs improve nutritional outcomes.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national school feeding coverage analysis for at least two low-income countries, with explicit documentation of coverage-data limitations, meal-quality variation, and the distinction between program existence and verified nutritional impact.
