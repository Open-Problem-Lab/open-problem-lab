# Birth Registration Coverage Gaps And Health-Service Linkage In Low-Income Countries

## Problem Statement

Birth registration looks simple until you try to use it operationally. The field uses at least three different things as if they were interchangeable:

- survey-based registration status for children under 5 at the time of interview
- administrative completeness of live-birth registration within a legal time window
- possession of documentary proof such as a birth certificate

Those are not the same measure. They move on different clocks, reflect different failure modes, and can point to different districts as "left behind". That is the real problem.

The second problem is practical rather than statistical. In many low-income countries, the state reaches infants through delivery care, postnatal care, immunization, and child-health services earlier and more consistently than the civil registry reaches them. That creates a tractable but under-specified workflow question:

> Which sub-national birth-registration gaps are measurable with enough rigor to support reviewable targeting, and where do health-system touchpoints offer the cheapest credible way to close those gaps?

This pack is not about producing a vanity map of "unregistered children". It is about separating indicator families, documenting where they disagree, and only then asking whether any health-service linkage claim deserves discussion.

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: UNICEF's December 2024 update reports that more than 200 million children under 5 lack a birth certificate globally, including 150 million whose births are unregistered and 55 million whose births are reported as registered but without documentary proof.
- Verified fact: UNICEF's birth-registration methodology states that in many low- and middle-income countries the main comparable source is household surveys, especially DHS and MICS, because functioning administrative registration systems remain incomplete.
- Verified fact: World Bank metadata for SDG indicator `SP.REG.BRTH.ZS` defines the common survey indicator as the share of children under age 5 whose births were registered at the time of the survey, and explicitly warns that this differs from CRVS measures of registered live births within a specific time frame.
- Verified fact: WHO's February 27, 2024 CRVS fact sheet states that only about half of countries have the capacity to register at least 90 percent of births and that the health sector is well placed to support registration because of its proximity to births and infant services.
- Verified fact: WHO and UNICEF's 2021 guidance for low-income countries treats health facilities, community health workers, immunization contact points, and birth notification workflows as practical levers for improving birth registration rather than merely monitoring it.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether comparable sub-national data exists for enough countries with both survey-based birth-registration measures and usable administrative or health-touchpoint data.
- Whether reported registration, certificate possession, and administrative completeness diverge so sharply in specific countries that any single composite ranking becomes misleading.
- Whether health-service linkage data is accessible enough to test "missed opportunity" claims rather than merely repeating them.
- Whether a sub-national gap artifact would change a real decision for a registrar, immunization manager, or child-health planner without creating coercive or exclusionary incentives.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory for survey-based birth-registration data, CRVS completeness data, certificate-possession measures, health-facility or immunization touchpoint data, and population denominators.
- Measurement notes that reconcile the difference between under-5 survey registration, proof-of-registration, and administrative live-birth completeness.
- Country selection memos that identify where sub-national work is genuinely possible rather than performatively global.
- Missed-opportunity analysis asking whether places with strong immunization or facility-birth reach still show birth-registration shortfalls.
- Red-team review of coercive targeting, denominator misuse, and overclaiming from incompatible measures.
- Field-reality review with civil registrars, immunization managers, and health-information officers.

This pack does not accept punitive enforcement ideas, legal advice, identity-system procurement recommendations, or claims that a registration gap map by itself justifies household-level intervention targeting.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national note for at least one low-income country that:

- states exactly which birth-registration measure is being used
- documents why that measure was chosen over alternatives
- names the health-system touchpoint being examined, if any
- identifies where the evidence is too weak for operational inference
