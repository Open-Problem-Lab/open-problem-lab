# Ambient Air Pollution Source Attribution And Health Impact In LMIC Megacities

## Problem Statement

Ambient air pollution causes an estimated 4.2 million premature deaths annually, with the burden concentrated in LMIC megacities where pollution levels far exceed WHO guidelines. But the dominant sources of that pollution — household solid-fuel combustion, vehicle exhaust, industrial emissions, agricultural burning, construction dust, and coal-fired power — vary dramatically by city. Delhi's PM2.5 source mix differs from Dhaka's, Lagos's, or Cairo's. Generic air-quality interventions (vehicle emission standards, industrial scrubbers) may be ineffective or counterproductive if the actual dominant sources are different.

Source-apportionment studies using chemical tracer analysis, receptor modeling, and satellite-based emission inventories can estimate the contribution of each source sector to ambient PM2.5 in a specific city. But source-apportionment studies are concentrated in high-income cities and Chinese megacities — LMIC megacities in South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and the Middle East have few or no published source-apportionment studies. The absence of source-attribution data means that air-quality policy in the most polluted cities is often based on assumptions borrowed from different contexts.

> Which LMIC megacities have the strongest evidence-backed source-attribution data for ambient PM2.5, and where are the evidence gaps most severe for air-quality policy?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: A 2021 systematic review by McDonald et al. found that household, traffic, industrial, and agricultural sources vary dramatically by city, with household solid-fuel combustion contributing 20-60 percent of PM2.5 in many South Asian cities versus 5-15 percent in most Sub-Saharan African cities where industrial and transport sources dominate.
- Verified fact: Source-apportionment methods (chemical tracer analysis, positive matrix factorization, chemical transport modeling) give different source-contribution estimates for the same city — methodology choice significantly affects attribution percentages.
- Verified fact: Satellite-derived PM2.5 estimates from instruments like MODIS, VIIRS, and TROPOMI provide spatial coverage where ground monitors are absent, but the satellite-to-ground PM2.5 transfer has uncertainty of 20-50 percent in LMIC cities with limited validation data.
- Verified fact: Ground air-quality monitoring in LMIC megacities is sparse relative to population — most South Asian and Sub-Saharan African megacities have fewer than 10 continuous PM2.5 monitoring stations for populations exceeding 10 million.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether source-apportionment results from a limited number of monitoring sites represent city-wide source contributions in LMIC megacities with heterogeneous land use and emission patterns.
- How seasonal variation in source contributions (e.g., biomass burning in winter, dust in summer) affects the representativeness of short-term source-apportionment campaigns.
- Whether source-attribution maps change actual air-quality policy, industrial regulation, or transport planning in LMIC cities with weak enforcement capacity.
- How informal economic activity (unregulated brick kilns, open waste burning, informal manufacturing) interacts with formal-sector source attribution in LMIC cities.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for source-apportionment studies, satellite PM2.5 products, and ground-monitor networks in LMIC megacities.
- Sub-national source-attribution mapping from published source-apportionment studies and satellite-derived emission inventories.
- Source-specific health-impact estimation using concentration-response functions applied to attributed source contributions.
- Red-team review of source-attribution overstatement and the risk of borrowed-context assumptions.
- Field-reality review with air-quality management agencies and health ministries in LMIC megacity contexts.

This pack does not accept source-specific emission standard recommendations, industrial closure proposals, or unvalidated source-attribution estimates for cities without published apportionment studies.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated source-attribution and health-impact analysis for at least two LMIC megacities, with explicit documentation of apportionment-methodology limitations, ground-monitor sparsity, and the distinction between published and borrowed source-attribution data.
