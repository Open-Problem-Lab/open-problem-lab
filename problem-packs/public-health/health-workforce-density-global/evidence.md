## Evidence Notes

### WHO Working for Health 2022-2030

Use this source for the global health workforce shortage baseline — 10 million health worker shortage projected by 2030, with 80 countries falling below the 4.45 doctors-nurses-midwives per 1,000 population density threshold. The shortage is concentrated in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia, which together account for over 70 percent of the projected gap and have the lowest health-worker production and retention rates.

The shortage projections depend on demand-modeling assumptions that may not account for task-shifting to community health workers, digital health tools, or changes in disease burden. Do not use the global 10-million figure as a substitute for sub-national workforce-distribution analysis. National health workforce registries exist in most countries but rarely include geo-located deployment data — payroll data is often the most practical source for sub-national workforce mapping.

### Skill-Mix Gap Context

Health workforce density alone understates the problem — the ratio of specialists to generalists, nurses to doctors, and community health workers to facility-based workers varies dramatically by district and is as important as absolute numbers for service delivery. Fewer than 30 countries report sub-national workforce distribution to the WHO National Health Workforce Accounts platform. Any workforce-gap analysis must document whether it measures density only or also captures skill-mix adequacy.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
