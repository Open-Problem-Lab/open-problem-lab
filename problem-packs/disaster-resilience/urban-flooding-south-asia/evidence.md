## Evidence Notes

### IPCC AR6 Urban Flooding 2022

Use this source for the regional risk baseline. The 2-3x drainage-capacity gap is a generalization — specific cities vary. Drainage-capacity data is inconsistently available. The IPCC assessment is city-level, not neighborhood-scale. Always document the gap between regional risk assessment and local drainage-data availability.

### Tellman et al. Satellite Flood Mapping 2021

Use satellite SAR flood mapping with the critical caveat that accuracy is reduced in urban canyons where building geometry creates radar shadow and layover. The 85 percent accuracy may be lower in dense urban areas. The 10m resolution may miss street-level flooding. SAR maps observed floods, not flood risk — historical extent does not predict future flooding. Always document urban-specific SAR limitations.

### Informal Settlement Flood Exposure

Use this source to document the disproportionate flood exposure of informal settlements and the critical risk that flood maps are used to justify eviction rather than infrastructure improvement. Settlement boundaries are often unmapped. Exposure is driven by multiple interacting factors. Always document the eviction-risk concern and present flood maps as tools for infrastructure prioritization, not displacement.

### Drainage Data Limitations

Use this source to document the fundamental data constraint: drainage-infrastructure data is outdated, incomplete, or nonexistent in most South Asian cities. OpenStreetMap coverage varies enormously. Municipal maps are often not publicly available. Drainage-capacity assessment requires pipe diameter, slope, and outfall data that is almost never available. Always document this gap explicitly in flood-risk analyses.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
