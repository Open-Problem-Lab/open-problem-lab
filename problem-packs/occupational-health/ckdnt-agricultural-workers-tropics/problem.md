# Chronic Kidney Disease Of Non-Traditional Etiology In Tropical Agricultural Workers

## Problem Statement

An epidemic of fatal chronic kidney disease is killing young agricultural workers in hot-climate regions across three continents. This is not conventional CKD from diabetes and hypertension, which typically affects older adults with metabolic risk factors. CKDnt strikes men and women in their twenties and thirties who work in sugarcane, cotton, rice, and other crops under extreme heat. The disease progresses silently, and most affected workers have no access to dialysis or transplantation. Death follows within months of diagnosis.

The cause is unknown. Two decades of research have produced competing hypotheses, and the scientific literature remains divided. The two most prominent are:

1. **Heat-dehydration hypothesis.** Repeated episodes of heat stress and dehydration during heavy physical labor in extreme temperatures cause kidney injury through volume depletion, hyperosmolarity, and uric acid crystallization. This pathway implicates working conditions and calls for occupational safety interventions: rest breaks, shade, hydration, and work schedule modification during peak heat.

2. **Agrochemical hypothesis.** Exposure to pesticides, herbicides, heavy metals, or contaminated water causes nephrotoxic kidney injury over months or years. This pathway implicates agricultural chemical regulation and water quality enforcement.

These are not mutually exclusive, and other hypotheses include infectious agents, genetic susceptibility, and fructose-uric acid metabolism. But the policy divergence is sharp: the heat hypothesis demands action from labor ministries and employers, while the agrochemical hypothesis demands action from environmental regulators and agricultural ministries. A government that splits its prevention budget evenly across both may save fewer lives than one that concentrates on the mechanism with the strongest evidence.

> Which etiology hypotheses for CKDnt have sufficient evidence to justify specific prevention policies, and which remain unproven enough that acting on them would be premature?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: A Seminars in Nephrology review by Correa-Rotter (2019) reports that Mesoamerican nephropathy is an epidemic of CKD affecting young agricultural workers in Central America, primarily sugarcane workers, with prevalence rates far exceeding conventional CKD, and that the etiology remains undetermined.
- Verified fact: A kidney biopsy study by Haas et al. in Kidney International (2018) found that Mesoamerican nephropathy shows a distinct pattern of chronic tubulointerstitial injury with glomerulosclerosis, supporting its classification as a distinct disease entity rather than a variant of conventional CKD.
- Verified fact: A prospective cohort study by Gonzalez-Quiroz et al. in the Journal of the American Society of Nephrology (2018) found measurable kidney function decline in apparently healthy young adults at risk of Mesoamerican nephropathy over two years, associated with agricultural work and heat exposure.
- Verified fact: A review by Wesseling et al. in the Pan American Journal of Public Health (2020) argues that CKDnt is primarily driven by occupational heat stress, citing evidence of recurrent dehydration and kidney injury markers in sugarcane workers.
- Verified fact: A systematic review in Environmental Health (2017) found that pesticide exposures are associated with CKDu in multiple studies, but most included studies use ecological exposure assessment and cannot separate pesticide exposure from heat stress.
- Verified fact: A study by Jayasumana et al. in Environmental Science and Technology Letters (2023) found that glyphosate and fluoride in high-hardness drinking water were positively associated with CKDu in Sri Lanka, providing ecological-level evidence for the agrochemical hypothesis.
- Verified fact: A study of dialysis enrollment patterns in Guatemala by Garcia-Trabanino et al. in BMC Nephrology (2015) found that CKDnt-affected regions had dialysis enrollment rates far exceeding national averages, with young male agricultural workers disproportionately represented.
- Verified fact: A 2026 review in Nature Reviews Nephrology by Osborne synthesizes environmental factors across affected regions and concludes that disentangling individual causal pathways remains the central challenge.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether heat-dehydration alone can cause the specific pattern of tubulointerstitial fibrosis seen in CKDnt, or whether a co-exposure is required.
- Whether the Mesoamerican, Sri Lankan, and Indian CKDnt cases share a single etiology or represent different diseases with similar presentation.
- Whether agrochemical exposure at the levels measured in affected communities is sufficient to cause the observed kidney injury, or whether it is a marker of proximity to industrial agriculture rather than a causal agent.
- Whether genetic susceptibility, infectious agents, or traditional medicine use contribute to the disease in specific populations.
- How CKDnt mortality and prevalence compare across affected regions when standardized to the same case definition and population denominator.
- Whether intervention trials for heat-stress reduction (hydration, rest breaks, shade) have been or could be designed to test the heat-dehydration hypothesis while controlling for agrochemical exposure.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory separating heat-dehydration, agrochemical, infectious, genetic, and mixed-cause hypotheses.
- Geographic mapping of CKDnt prevalence and mortality by country, region, and worker population.
- Case definition harmonization across Mesoamerican, Sri Lankan, and Indian study designs.
- Dataset inventory for heat exposure, agrochemical exposure, water quality, kidney function biomarkers, and worker population denominators.
- Systematic comparison of exposure-outcome evidence quality across hypotheses.
- Red-team review of premature causal claims and policy recommendations that exceed the evidence.
- Field-reality review with occupational health agencies, nephrology clinicians, and agricultural labor organizations.

This pack does not accept direct medical advice, individual treatment recommendations, pesticide ban advocacy without evidence, or employer liability claims.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated evidence synthesis that:

- separates the heat-dehydration and agrochemical hypotheses by evidence quality
- names the specific exposure-outcome studies that support or weaken each hypothesis
- identifies the decision-maker and decision that each hypothesis would change
- states explicitly which prevention policies are evidence-backed and which remain premature
