# Neonatal Mortality And Inpatient Newborn Care Quality

## Problem Statement

2.3 million newborns died in their first month of life in 2022 — nearly half of all under-five deaths. The majority died from three preventable causes: prematurity complications, birth asphyxia, and neonatal sepsis. While facility delivery rates have increased substantially across low-income countries, the quality of inpatient newborn care has not kept pace. Service Provision Assessment surveys reveal that even facilities classified as providing comprehensive emergency obstetric and newborn care frequently lack functional resuscitation equipment, continuous positive airway pressure, thermal care, and intravenous antibiotics — the seven signal functions that distinguish a facility capable of saving a sick newborn from one that cannot.

> Which sub-national areas have the highest evidence-backed gap between neonatal mortality rates and the quality of inpatient newborn care available within accessible travel time?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: UNICEF's 2024 child mortality report estimates 2.3 million neonatal deaths globally in 2022, with Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia accounting for over 80 percent, and three causes — prematurity (35 percent), birth asphyxia (20 percent), and sepsis (15 percent) — responsible for 70 percent of deaths.
- Verified fact: Service Provision Assessment surveys in 12 low-income countries document that fewer than 30 percent of facilities classified as providing inpatient newborn care possess all seven signal functions: resuscitation, CPAP, thermal care, IV fluids, IV antibiotics, phototherapy, and safe oxygen delivery.
- Verified fact: The 2014 Every Newborn Lancet series estimated that universal coverage of kangaroo mother care, neonatal resuscitation, and infection management could prevent 71 percent of neonatal deaths, but actual coverage of these interventions remains below 40 percent in most high-burden districts.
- Verified fact: DHS neonatal mortality modules and SPA facility assessments exist for overlapping countries and years, enabling sub-national linkage of mortality rates to facility-readiness indicators at district scale for the first time.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether SPA facility-assessment data from 2013-2022 can be assumed current for facilities not re-surveyed. Many facilities classified as providing newborn care in older surveys may have deteriorated or improved without documentation.
- Whether travel-time catchments based on facility administrative boundaries accurately reflect where women with sick newborns actually seek care, given bypassing behavior toward referral hospitals.
- How community-based newborn care delivered by health extension workers and community health workers interacts with facility-based quality in determining mortality outcomes.
- Whether quality-gap maps change procurement and training investments versus being used punitively to close or downgrade struggling facilities.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for neonatal mortality and facility-readiness data.
- Sub-national neonatal mortality estimation using DHS data with appropriate confidence intervals for small populations.
- Inpatient newborn care quality classification from SPA and similar facility-assessment data.
- Facility-to-population access mapping using travel-time surfaces and facility coordinates.
- Red-team review of quality-gap communication, including risks of punitive facility closures and false precision in small-area mortality estimates.
- Field-reality review with district health management teams and newborn-care program managers.

This pack does not accept direct facility-closure recommendations, procurement mandates for specific device brands, or unvalidated mortality-attribution claims.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national analysis linking district-level neonatal mortality to inpatient newborn care quality, with explicit travel-time-adjusted access estimates and facility-data-currency caveats, that a domain reviewer and field-reality reviewer judge suitable for quality-improvement discussion rather than facility-designation decisions.
