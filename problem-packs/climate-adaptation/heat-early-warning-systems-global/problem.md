# Heat Early Warning System Coverage And Effectiveness In Low-Income Countries

## Problem Statement

Heatwaves are the deadliest weather-related hazard globally, causing more fatalities than floods, cyclones, and earthquakes combined in many years. The World Meteorological Organization estimates that fewer than 40 low- and middle-income countries have operational heat-health early warning systems, and where systems exist, systematic evaluation of their mortality-reduction effectiveness is almost entirely absent. High-income countries have demonstrated that heat early warning combined with cooling-center activation, public communication, and targeted outreach to vulnerable populations can reduce heat-attributable mortality by 20-50 percent. But the translation of these systems to LMIC contexts — where air conditioning is rare, outdoor work is unavoidable, and health-system surge capacity is limited — faces fundamentally different constraints.

WMO's State of Climate Services reports track whether countries have heat-health warning systems, but the binary existence indicator does not capture whether warnings reach vulnerable populations, trigger protective actions, or reduce mortality. Sub-national heat-mortality data is sparse in LMICs because vital registration systems are incomplete and heat-attributable deaths are rarely coded as such. The gap between nominal warning-system existence and verified mortality-reduction effectiveness is the core measurement problem.

> Which LMICs and sub-national areas have the strongest evidence-backed gap between heat-health warning system existence and demonstrated mortality-reduction effectiveness?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: WMO's State of Climate Services 2023 report on health estimates that fewer than 40 LMICs have operational heat-health early warning systems. Where systems exist, systematic evaluation of mortality-reduction effectiveness is rare — fewer than 10 countries globally have published heat-wave mortality time series with pre- and post-warning-system comparison.
- Verified fact: Heatwaves are the deadliest weather hazard in many high-income countries with good vital registration, but mortality data from LMICs systematically undercounts heat deaths because cause-of-death certification rarely attributes mortality to heat exposure.
- Verified fact: Ahmedabad's Heat Action Plan (India, 2013) is the most cited LMIC heat early warning example, with estimated mortality reduction of approximately 1,100 deaths per year. The evaluation methodology compared pre- and post-implementation mortality during heat waves but relied on all-cause mortality data rather than heat-specific cause-of-death coding.
- Verified fact: The Red Cross Red Crescent Climate Centre and WMO have developed forecast-based financing mechanisms for heatwaves that release pre-agreed funds when temperature thresholds are crossed, but the number of countries with operational heat-related forecast-based financing protocols is under five.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether heat-mortality data from LMIC vital registration systems is adequate for warning-system effectiveness evaluation, or whether alternative mortality-data sources (burial records, sentinel surveillance) must be used.
- How to measure warning-system effectiveness when heatwaves are increasing in frequency and intensity — historical baselines are shifting, making pre-post comparisons unreliable without climate-trend adjustment.
- Whether the effectiveness of heat early warning systems in high-income urban contexts (Paris, Chicago, Melbourne) generalizes to LMIC settings with informal settlements, outdoor labor, and limited cooling infrastructure.
- How warning-system coverage interacts with the actual availability of cooling centers, water, and emergency medical services to produce mortality outcomes — a system that warns without enabling protective action may be worse than no system at all.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for heat-wave mortality data, heat early warning system coverage data, and forecast-based financing program data.
- Heat early warning system coverage mapping at national and sub-national scale using WMO, IFRC, and national meteorological agency data.
- Heat-mortality trend analysis comparing regions with and without warning systems, with explicit climate-trend adjustment.
- Red-team review of warning-system effectiveness overstatement and the risk of attributing mortality reduction to warning systems when secular trends in healthcare, housing, and air conditioning may explain the improvement.
- Field-reality review with national meteorological services and disaster-management authorities.

This pack does not accept heat early warning system design recommendations, temperature-threshold proposals for specific cities, or unvalidated mortality-reduction claims.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national analysis of heat early warning system coverage and heat-mortality trends for at least two LMICs, with explicit documentation of mortality-data quality, climate-trend adjustment methodology, and the distinction between system existence and verified effectiveness.
