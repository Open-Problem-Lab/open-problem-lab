# Playbooks

## Analyst Playbook

1. Start with `problem.json`, `evidence.json`, and `tasks.json`.
2. Select one scoped task.
3. Add evidence before adding claims.
4. For fertilizer-access mapping: always document whether data is national aggregate or sub-national — continental averages (15 kg/ha) mask enormous country and district variation. Document distribution-infrastructure barriers including transport costs and price markups.
5. For soil-nutrient analysis: always document AfSIS coverage limitations — data is concentrated at research sites with spatial interpolation between them. Nutrient depletion rates require repeated measurements available at very few sites.
6. For response-variability: always document that degraded soils show 30-50 percent lower fertilizer response — soil-specific recommendations are essential, and blanket fertilizer advice without soil analysis may waste resources.
7. Mark every operational inference as reviewed, replicated, or blocked.

## Reviewer Playbook

Ask five questions:

1. What claim is being made about fertilizer access or soil nutrient depletion?
2. What source proves it — and is it national survey data, AfSIS soil data, or fertilizer-response trials?
3. What method translates fertilizer-use data into access-gap claims, and what is the sub-national data availability?
4. What would make it false — particularly if national averages mask sub-national targeting needs, if AfSIS interpolation doesn't represent local soil conditions, or if response rates in degraded soils don't match research-station results?
5. Who could misuse it — particularly to justify blanket fertilizer subsidies without soil-specific recommendations, to overstate access gaps using continental averages, or to ignore distribution-infrastructure barriers?

## Field-Reality Playbook

Do not ask whether a fertilizer-access gap map is impressive. Ask whether it would change a named decision for a named user at a useful time, without creating worse incentives. For fertilizer access in SSA, ask specifically:

- Would a Kenya Ministry of Agriculture soil-fertility officer use this to target fertilizer subsidies by soil type, or would continental averages mask the actual district-level access patterns?
- Would a fertilizer distributor use this to identify underserved markets, or would distribution-infrastructure barriers (transport costs, price markups) make remote areas commercially unviable without subsidy?
- Would a soil-health program use this to prioritize intervention areas, or would AfSIS interpolation limitations mean the map doesn't reflect actual soil conditions at the target sites?

The answer must include explicit non-use cases before any field-facing output is published.
