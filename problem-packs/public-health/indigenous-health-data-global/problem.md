# Indigenous Health Data Sovereignty And Service Access Gaps

## Problem Statement

Indigenous populations experience a life-expectancy gap of 5-20 years compared to non-indigenous populations in every country where data exists. Australia, Canada, New Zealand, the United States, and multiple Latin American countries have documented this gap, but in most Sub-Saharan African and Asian countries, indigenous identifiers are absent from health information systems entirely. Fewer than 30 countries include indigenous identifiers in routine health data, and fewer than 10 operate indigenous-governed health data systems.

The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, and Ethics — have been endorsed by the UN Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues and major research-funding bodies. These principles establish that indigenous peoples must govern the collection, ownership, and application of data about their communities. The core methodological challenge is not technical but political and ethical: measuring health-service access gaps while respecting indigenous data sovereignty and rejecting extractive research practices.

> How can sub-national indigenous health-service access gaps be measured while respecting CARE principles and indigenous data governance, such that any analysis is co-designed with and governed by the communities whose data it uses?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: The UN Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues documents life-expectancy gaps of 5-20 years between indigenous and non-indigenous populations in Australia (approximately 8 years), Canada (approximately 10-15 years), New Zealand (approximately 7 years), the United States, and across Latin American countries. Data for Africa and Asia is almost entirely absent.
- Verified fact: Fewer than 30 countries include indigenous identifiers in routine health data collection, and fewer than 10 have health data systems governed by indigenous data-sovereignty protocols aligned with CARE principles.
- Verified fact: The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics) were developed by the Global Indigenous Data Alliance and have been formally endorsed by the UN Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues and research-funding bodies including the Wellcome Trust.
- Verified fact: New Zealand's Whānau Ora program and Australia's Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Organisations provide working models of indigenous-governed health-service delivery that include indigenous-controlled health data collection and reporting.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether indigenous identifiers can be added to existing national health information systems without reinforcing colonial data-extraction patterns — versus requiring entirely indigenous-governed parallel systems.
- How sub-national analysis granularity should be determined by indigenous communities themselves rather than by researcher convenience or statistical power calculations.
- Whether health-access gap analyses change indigenous health-resource allocation when the same governments that created the gaps control the resources.
- How the tension between open-data norms and indigenous data-sovereignty requirements should be resolved in the context of a public protocol like Open Problem Lab.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment conducted with indigenous data-governance consultation.
- Documentation of existing indigenous-governed health-data systems as models for sovereignty-respecting measurement.
- Community-governed methodology development for health-access gap measurement.
- Red-team review of colonial data-extraction risks in any proposed measurement framework.
- Field-reality review co-conducted with indigenous health-governance authorities.

This pack does not accept health-data analysis that lacks documented indigenous governance approval, or recommendations about indigenous health policy that originate outside indigenous governance structures.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated indigenous health-access measurement framework that is co-designed with an indigenous data-governance authority, documents CARE-principles compliance for every data source, and includes explicit indigenous-community sign-off on publication scope and limits.
