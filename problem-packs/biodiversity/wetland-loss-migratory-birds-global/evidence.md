## Evidence Notes

### Ramsar Wetland Loss 2023

Use this source for the global wetland loss baseline. The 35 percent figure is a global aggregate — regional and local loss rates vary substantially. Wetland mapping methodology varies by country and time period, so the figure combines multiple approaches. Do not use this as proof of wetland loss at any specific stopover site without satellite verification from JRC or equivalent products.

### JRC Global Surface Water

Use JRC as the primary satellite product for wetland-change detection at stopover sites. The 30m resolution may miss small ephemeral wetlands critical for shorebird stopover. Intertidal wetland detection is complicated by tidal cycles — tidal state, not actual loss, may explain apparent water-land transitions. The product classifies surface water presence, not wetland habitat quality — a persisting but degraded water body is not captured as habitat loss. Always document these limitations in flyway-scale analyses.

### East Asian-Australasian Flyway Decline

Use this source for the strongest documented case of wetland-loss-driven migratory bird decline. The 50 percent decline is an aggregate across multiple species with individual trends ranging from stable to over 80 percent decline. Attribution to Yellow Sea wetland loss is complicated by other flyway-wide threats — hunting in Southeast Asia, disturbance at Australian sites, climate change on Arctic breeding grounds. Do not assume the Yellow Sea attribution holds for all declining species.

### IWC and eBird Coverage

Use this source to document the fundamental data constraint: bird-population monitoring is concentrated in Europe and North America, with sparser coverage along Asian and African flyways where wetland loss is fastest. Long-term trend analysis requires consistent monitoring effort available at few Asian or African sites. eBird data has severe spatial bias toward accessible sites. Document this coverage asymmetry explicitly in every flyway-scale analysis.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
