# Mini-Grid Rural Electrification Viability In Sub-Saharan Africa

## Problem Statement

Over 600 million people in Sub-Saharan Africa lack electricity. Solar-hybrid mini-grids can power rural health clinics, schools, and productive uses at lower cost than grid extension. But site selection requires stacking settlement locations, solar irradiance, demand estimates, and cost data — each with its own uncertainty. When viability maps are wrong, scarce investment goes to the wrong places, or viable communities are left unserved.

> Which sub-national mini-grid viability signals are evidence-backed enough to support electrification planning without overstating site-level certainty?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: IEA estimates over 600 million people in Sub-Saharan Africa lack electricity, with rural electrification rates below 30 percent in most countries.
- Verified fact: Solar PV costs have fallen 90 percent since 2010 and solar-hybrid mini-grids can provide 24/7 electricity at lower levelized cost than diesel generators for most rural communities.
- Verified fact: The World Bank's ESMAP program has funded mini-grid feasibility mapping in over 20 countries, but maps vary substantially in methodology and accuracy.
- Verified fact: Settlement location data from satellite imagery and census sources is improving but misses informal settlements and may misclassify building density.

## Uncertain Areas

Whether solar resource data at 10km resolution is sufficient for site-level mini-grid sizing, how demand estimation for un-electrified communities can be validated without baseline consumption data, and whether viability maps change actual investment decisions.

## Initial Scope

Evidence inventory, dataset availability, viability-factor spatial inventory, multi-criteria viability ranking with uncertainty, red-team review, field-reality review.

## Done Condition For V0

One reviewed and replicated sub-national mini-grid viability analysis for at least two countries with explicit uncertainty documentation.
