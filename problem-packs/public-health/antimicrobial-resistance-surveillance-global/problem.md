# Antimicrobial Resistance Surveillance Gaps In LMICs

## Problem Statement

AMR killed 1.27 million people directly in 2019, with the highest burden in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. WHO GLASS collects surveillance from 127 countries but sub-national coverage is sparse — laboratories are concentrated in capitals. Linking GLASS data to lab-capacity maps reveals where the AMR burden is highest but invisible to surveillance.

> Which sub-national areas in LMICs have the highest evidence-backed AMR surveillance gaps relative to estimated AMR burden?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: The Lancet AMR study estimated 1.27 million deaths directly attributable to AMR and 4.95 million associated deaths in 2019, with the highest burden in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia.
- Verified fact: WHO GLASS reports AMR surveillance data from 127 countries but fewer than 40 countries report sub-national data and fewer than 20 meet quality standards for bloodstream infection surveillance.
- Verified fact: Laboratory capacity for bacterial culture and susceptibility testing is concentrated in capital cities — most district hospitals in LMICs lack any AMR diagnostic capacity.
- Verified fact: Antibiotic consumption data is sparse in LMICs, with fewer than 30 countries reporting to WHO's antimicrobial consumption monitoring system.

## Uncertain Areas

Whether GLASS enrollment predicts actual AMR surveillance capacity. Whether lab-capacity maps can proxy for surveillance gaps. Whether gap maps change laboratory-investment decisions.

## Initial Scope

Evidence inventory, GLASS data compilation, lab-capacity mapping, surveillance-gap analysis, red-team review, field-reality review.

## Done Condition For V0

One reviewed and replicated sub-national AMR surveillance gap analysis for at least two LMICs.
