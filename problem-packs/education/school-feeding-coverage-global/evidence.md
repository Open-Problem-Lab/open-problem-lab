## Evidence Notes

### WFP School Feeding 2024

Use this source for the global coverage baseline. The 418 million figure combines programs of vastly different quality — daily nutritious meals to occasional snacks — without distinguishing nutritional adequacy. Coverage data depends on country self-reporting with limited verification. Sub-national variation is rarely documented. Always present coverage with program-quality context and explicitly state where sub-national data is absent.

### School Feeding Enrollment Effects

Use this source for the strongest evidence on school feeding effectiveness — enrollment and attendance effects are well-documented at 8-12 and 5-9 percentage points respectively. However, these are pooled averages across diverse programs. Enrollment effects may be driven by the feeding incentive rather than nutritional improvement. Always present with heterogeneity documentation and distinguish the enrollment channel from the nutrition channel.

### Nutritional Impact Evidence

Use this source to document that nutritional-impact evidence is weaker than enrollment evidence. Meal quality varies enormously. Anthropometric effects are inconsistent across studies. Many studies do not control for concurrent nutrition interventions. Always present nutritional-impact claims with explicit uncertainty and meal-quality variation context.

### School Feeding Cost-Effectiveness

Use this source for cost context. The $0.10-$0.50 range depends on program design and local food prices. Cost-effectiveness comparisons with alternative interventions are sensitive to outcome measure. Always present cost comparisons with explicit outcome-measure and context documentation.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
