# Playbooks

## Analyst Playbook

1. Start with `problem.json`, `evidence.json`, and `tasks.json`.
2. Select one scoped task.
3. Add evidence before adding claims.
4. For crop-pollination dependence mapping: always document the source of each crop's pollination-dependence classification and whether it was derived from temperate or tropical studies.
5. For habitat-change analysis: always document that satellite land-cover trends are proxies for pollinator habitat pressure, not direct measurements of pollinator abundance. Document the absence of ground-truth pollinator monitoring in the analyzed region.
6. For risk mapping: always present results as proxy-based risk indicators with explicit monitoring-gap caveats, not as verified decline measurements.
7. Mark every operational inference as reviewed, replicated, or blocked.

## Reviewer Playbook

Ask five questions:

1. What claim is being made about pollinator decline or crop-pollination service risk?
2. What source proves it — and is it direct pollinator monitoring, a proxy indicator, or an assumption transferred from a different region?
3. What method separates pollinator-habitat pressure from other crop-production drivers (climate, soil, management)?
4. What would make it false — particularly if the proxy-to-pollinator inference doesn't hold in the analyzed region, or if pollination-dependence classifications from temperate studies don't transfer to tropical crops?
5. Who could misuse it — particularly to justify pesticide bans without direct evidence, overstate crop-loss risk, or undermine farmer confidence in natural pollination services?

## Field-Reality Playbook

Do not ask whether a pollination-service risk map is impressive. Ask whether it would change a named decision for a named user at a useful time, without creating worse incentives. For pollinator decline, ask specifically:

- Would a national agricultural research institute entomologist use this to prioritize pollinator monitoring site selection, or would it be treated as a verified decline measurement when no monitoring data exists?
- Would an agricultural extension officer use this to recommend pollinator-friendly farming practices, or would it be used to justify pesticide bans without direct evidence of pollinator harm?
- Would a conservation program use this to target habitat restoration, or would it divert resources from more evidence-based conservation priorities?

The answer must include explicit non-use cases before any field-facing output is published.
