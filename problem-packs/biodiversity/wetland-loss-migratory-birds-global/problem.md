# Wetland Loss Detection And Migratory Bird Population Impact

## Problem Statement

The Ramsar Convention's Global Wetland Outlook estimates that 35 percent of global wetlands have been lost since 1970 — a rate three times faster than forest loss — with the fastest losses occurring in Asia and Africa. Wetlands provide critical stopover habitat for migratory birds on the East Asian-Australasian Flyway, the Central Asian Flyway, and the African-Eurasian Flyway, along with water purification, flood control, and carbon storage. The loss of coastal and inland wetlands has been directly linked to population declines in migratory shorebirds, waterfowl, and other wetland-dependent species, with some shorebird populations declining by more than 50 percent in the past two decades.

Satellite-derived wetland maps from the Global Surface Water Explorer (JRC) at 30m resolution provide monthly surface-water observations from 1984 to present, enabling detection of permanent wetland loss, seasonal water-body changes, and conversion to agriculture or urban land. The Ramsar Sites Information Service catalogs protected wetlands, but many critical stopover sites — particularly in the Yellow Sea region of East Asia — lie outside formal protection. Linking satellite wetland-change data to migratory bird population counts from the International Waterbird Census and eBird data at flyway scale is the underdeveloped analytical chain.

> Which wetland stopover sites along major migratory flyways have the strongest evidence-backed link between satellite-detected wetland loss and migratory bird population decline?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: The Ramsar Global Wetland Outlook 2023 reports 35 percent of global wetlands lost since 1970, with the fastest losses in Asia (driven by coastal reclamation and aquaculture) and Africa (driven by agricultural expansion and water diversion).
- Verified fact: JRC Global Surface Water Explorer provides monthly surface-water observations at 30m resolution from 1984 to present from Landsat imagery, covering all inland and coastal water bodies larger than 30x30 meters.
- Verified fact: The International Waterbird Census and eBird provide population-count data for migratory waterbirds at thousands of sites globally, but data is concentrated in well-monitored sites in Europe and North America with sparser coverage along Asian and African flyways.
- Verified fact: Shorebird populations on the East Asian-Australasian Flyway have declined by over 50 percent since the 1990s, primarily due to the loss of intertidal wetlands in the Yellow Sea region from coastal reclamation.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether 30m-resolution satellite wetland maps adequately capture small, ephemeral wetlands that provide critical stopover habitat for migratory birds.
- How to attribute migratory bird population declines to specific wetland-loss events along flyways when birds are exposed to multiple threats across their migratory range.
- Whether wetland-loss maps at flyway scale change conservation-priority designation or international wetland-protection agreements.

## Initial Scope

Evidence inventory, satellite wetland-change mapping, bird-population correlation, flyway-scale risk analysis, red-team review, field-reality review.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated flyway-scale analysis linking satellite-detected wetland loss to migratory bird population trends for at least one major flyway, with explicit wetland-bird linkage methodology and data-coverage documentation.
