# Grassland Degradation Detection And Pastoral Livelihood Risk In Central Asia

## Problem Statement

Central Asia's steppes and rangelands — stretching from Kazakhstan through Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, Kyrgyzstan, and Mongolia — support millions of pastoralists whose livelihoods depend on grassland productivity for livestock grazing. The FAO estimates that 20-30 percent of global grasslands are degraded, with Central Asian rangelands experiencing some of the highest degradation rates worldwide due to overgrazing, climate change, and the legacy of Soviet-era collectivization followed by rapid destocking and institutional collapse in the 1990s. Degradation reduces forage availability, increases livestock mortality during harsh winters (dzud events in Mongolia), and drives pastoralist impoverishment and out-migration.

Satellite-derived vegetation indices — particularly MODIS NDVI at 250m resolution — can detect grassland productivity trends over decades, and products like the Rangeland Analysis Platform use Landsat time series to map fractional vegetation cover change. But linking satellite-detected degradation signals to ground-verified forage availability, livestock body condition, and pastoralist livelihood outcomes at sub-national scale remains underdeveloped. Degradation is not uniform: localized areas around wells and settlements may be severely degraded while remote pastures remain productive, and the spatial scale of NDVI change detection may not match herder decision-making scales.

> Which sub-national areas in Central Asia have the strongest evidence-backed grassland degradation signals from satellite data, and how do these signals correlate with pastoralist livelihood outcomes?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: The FAO Status of the World's Grasslands estimates that 20-30 percent of global grasslands are degraded, with Central Asian steppes and rangelands experiencing some of the highest degradation rates globally. Degradation is driven primarily by overgrazing, conversion to cropland, and climate-change-induced aridification.
- Verified fact: MODIS NDVI at 250m resolution provides continuous vegetation-productivity monitoring from 2000 to present, enabling trend analysis of grassland condition. The Rangeland Analysis Platform and similar products map fractional vegetation cover change at 30m resolution from Landsat data.
- Verified fact: Mongolia's dzud events — extreme winters following dry summers that prevent livestock from accessing forage — killed over 10 million livestock in the 2009-2010 dzud alone, and climate change is increasing dzud frequency.
- Verified fact: Central Asian pastoralist populations are among the most data-sparse in the world — livestock census data is irregular, household-level livelihood data is infrequent, and pastoralist mobility makes conventional survey sampling difficult.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether satellite NDVI trends at 250m resolution accurately reflect forage availability at the paddock scale relevant to herder decision-making, particularly in heterogeneous rangelands.
- How to distinguish climate-driven productivity variability (which may recover in wet years) from persistent degradation (which does not) in NDVI time series analysis.
- Whether grassland degradation maps change rangeland management policy, livestock-insurance program design, or pastoralist mobility patterns.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on: evidence inventory, satellite vegetation-trend analysis, degradation classification, pastoralist-livelihood correlation analysis, red-team review, field-reality review.

This pack does not accept grazing-policy recommendations, livestock-insurance product designs, or unvalidated degradation-attribution claims.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national grassland degradation and pastoralist-livelihood risk analysis for at least two Central Asian countries, with explicit NDVI-trend methodology and ground-validation documentation.
