# Digital Divide Measurement And School Internet Connectivity In Low-Income Countries

## Problem Statement

UNICEF and ITU estimate 1.3 billion school-age children — two-thirds globally — have no internet at home. Two-thirds of schools in low-income countries lack any internet connection. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed this divide catastrophically: when schools closed, children without connectivity lost access to education entirely. But the digital divide is not binary — it spans from no connectivity to intermittent mobile coverage to reliable broadband, and from no devices to shared devices to one-to-one access. Measuring this spectrum at school level is the prerequisite for evidence-targeted infrastructure investment.

School-level connectivity data comes from two main sources: Giga (a UNICEF-ITU initiative mapping 1 million+ schools across 30 countries using satellite imagery and ML) and national school census ICT indicators. But these sources use different connectivity definitions, have different update frequencies, and rarely capture the quality dimension — bandwidth, reliability, and actual usage. The gap between connectivity existence and verified learning-use is the core measurement problem.

> Which sub-national districts in low-income countries have the strongest evidence-backed school connectivity gaps, and where is the data most insufficient?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: UNICEF and ITU estimate 1.3 billion school-age children lack home internet access and two-thirds of schools in low-income countries lack any internet connection, with the divide concentrated in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia.
- Verified fact: Giga has mapped school locations and connectivity status for over 1 million schools across 30 countries using satellite imagery and ML to identify school locations and classify connectivity, providing the most comprehensive school-connectivity dataset available.
- Verified fact: School census data in many low-income countries includes ICT indicators — computer availability, internet connectivity, electricity — but data currency varies widely (some countries use census data from 5+ years ago) and is not systematically cross-validated against connectivity measurements.
- Verified fact: Connectivity alone does not close the digital divide — electricity availability, device access, teacher digital literacy, and relevant digital content are all necessary conditions that connectivity maps do not capture.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether satellite-derived connectivity classifications (from Giga) match ground-truthed school reports, particularly for intermittent or mobile-only connectivity.
- Whether connectivity maps translate into actual infrastructure investment targeting, or whether investment decisions are driven by political and commercial factors that maps cannot influence.
- How the quality dimension — bandwidth, reliability, and actual educational usage — varies across connected schools.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for Giga school-connectivity data, national school census ICT indicators, mobile-network coverage maps, and connectivity-quality studies.
- Sub-national school-connectivity gap mapping using Giga data and school census data.
- Connectivity-quality analysis documenting bandwidth, reliability, and usage where data exists.
- Red-team review of connectivity-map overstatement and the risk that connectivity is treated as sufficient for digital learning without electricity, devices, and teacher training.
- Field-reality review with education ministry ICT directors and school-connectivity program managers.

This pack does not accept technology procurement recommendations, connectivity-technology choices (satellite vs. fiber vs. mobile), or unvalidated claims that connectivity alone improves learning outcomes.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national school-connectivity gap analysis for at least two low-income countries, with explicit documentation of connectivity-classification methodology, data currency, and the distinction between connectivity existence and verified digital-learning use.
