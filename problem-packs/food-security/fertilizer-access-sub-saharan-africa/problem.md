# Fertilizer Access And Soil Nutrient Depletion In Smallholder Agriculture In Sub-Saharan Africa

## Problem Statement

Sub-Saharan Africa's smallholder farmers use an average of 15 kg of fertilizer per hectare — one-tenth of the global average of 150 kg/ha. Soil nutrient depletion costs an estimated 3 percent of agricultural GDP annually, degrading the productive base that 600 million Africans depend on for food and livelihoods. The fertilizer access gap is not simply about supply — it reflects a complex interaction of affordability, distribution infrastructure, soil-specific recommendation systems, credit access, and the economics of fertilizer response in degraded soils.

Fertilizer-use data comes from national agricultural surveys and the FAO, but sub-national variation is enormous — districts near transport corridors may have near-commercial fertilizer access while remote areas have none. Soil nutrient data from AfSIS and national soil surveys provides the scientific basis for soil-specific fertilizer recommendations, but coverage outside research sites is sparse. The gap between fertilizer availability and verified soil-nutrient improvement is the core measurement problem.

> Which sub-national districts in SSA have the strongest evidence-backed gap between fertilizer access and soil nutrient depletion, and where are the data most insufficient?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: The 2023 Africa Fertilizer and Soil Health Summit documented average fertilizer use of 15 kg/ha in SSA versus 150 kg/ha globally, with soil nutrient depletion costing an estimated 3 percent of agricultural GDP annually.
- Verified fact: Soil nutrient data from AfSIS and national soil surveys shows severe nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium depletion across much of SSA's cropland, with nutrient mining (removal exceeding application) documented in most smallholder farming systems.
- Verified fact: Fertilizer distribution infrastructure in SSA is concentrated near transport corridors and urban centers — remote smallholder areas face fertilizer price markups of 50-200 percent over international reference prices due to transport costs and distribution inefficiencies.
- Verified fact: Fertilizer response rates in degraded soils are often 30-50 percent lower than in well-managed soils, meaning that fertilizer investment returns are context-dependent and soil-specific recommendations are essential for cost-effectiveness.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether sub-national fertilizer-use data exists at sufficient detail for targeting analysis, or whether only national aggregate statistics are available in most SSA countries.
- How soil-specific fertilizer recommendations interact with farmer affordability — optimal application rates may be economically inaccessible for resource-poor smallholders.
- Whether fertilizer-access gap maps change subsidy program targeting, distribution-investment decisions, or soil-health program design.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for fertilizer-use data, soil nutrient surveys, distribution-infrastructure data, and fertilizer-response studies.
- Sub-national fertilizer-access mapping using agricultural survey data and distribution-infrastructure analysis.
- Soil nutrient depletion analysis linking soil survey data to fertilizer application rates and crop response.
- Red-team review of fertilizer-access overstatement and the risk that blanket fertilizer recommendations ignore soil-specific response variability.
- Field-reality review with agricultural ministries, fertilizer distributors, and soil health programs.

This pack does not accept fertilizer-subsidy recommendations, specific product formulations, or unvalidated claims that increasing fertilizer use alone improves soil health without soil-specific recommendations.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national fertilizer-access and soil-nutrient analysis for at least two SSA countries, with explicit documentation of fertilizer-use data limitations, distribution-infrastructure barriers, and soil-specific response variability.
