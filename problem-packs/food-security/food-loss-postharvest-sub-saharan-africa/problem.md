# Postharvest Food Loss Measurement And Reduction Potential In Sub-Saharan Africa

## Problem Statement

FAO estimates 30-40 percent of food produced in Sub-Saharan Africa is lost postharvest — enough to feed 300 million people. Losses are highest at farm-to-market stages where storage, cold chains, and processing infrastructure are weakest. Hermetic storage bags and metal silos can reduce on-farm storage losses by 50-80 percent, but adoption is below 10 percent in most SSA countries. Cold-chain infrastructure for perishable crops is almost absent outside major urban corridors.

Postharvest loss data comes from APHLIS (cereals at provincial scale) and the FAO Food Loss Index (country-level aggregates), but these sources use different methodologies and cover different crops. Household-survey food-loss modules provide direct measurement but are available in only a few countries. The gap between modeled loss estimates and verified household-level measurement is the core measurement problem.

> Which sub-national areas in SSA have the highest evidence-backed postharvest food loss, and where would storage-infrastructure investment reduce losses most?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: FAO estimates 30-40 percent of food produced in SSA is lost postharvest, concentrated at handling, storage, and transport stages, with cereals and perishable crops most affected.
- Verified fact: APHLIS provides postharvest loss estimates for maize, sorghum, millet, and other cereals at provincial scale across SSA, with losses ranging from 10-30 percent by crop and location.
- Verified fact: Hermetic storage bags and metal silos can reduce on-farm storage losses by 50-80 percent, but adoption is below 10 percent in most SSA countries due to cost, awareness, and distribution barriers.
- Verified fact: Cold-chain infrastructure for perishable crops (fruits, vegetables, dairy) is almost absent outside major urban corridors, making perishable crop losses 30-50 percent higher than cereal losses in most SSA contexts.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether APHLIS provincial estimates can be validated with household-level food-loss measurement where both exist.
- Whether storage-infrastructure maps predict actual loss levels, or whether farmer behavior and market access are more important determinants.
- Whether loss-reduction maps change storage-technology investment targeting, cold-chain development, or food-system policy decisions.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for APHLIS loss estimates, FAO Food Loss Index, household-survey food-loss modules, and storage-infrastructure data.
- Sub-national postharvest loss mapping using APHLIS and FAO data for major staple crops.
- Loss-reduction gap analysis identifying where storage-technology adoption would reduce losses most.
- Red-team review of loss-estimate overstatement and the risk that modeled estimates are treated as verified measurements.
- Field-reality review with food-system planners, storage-technology programs, and agricultural ministries.

This pack does not accept storage-technology product recommendations, cold-chain investment proposals, or unvalidated claims that specific interventions reduce losses by specific percentages.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national postharvest food loss analysis for at least two SSA countries, with explicit documentation of loss-estimation methodology, crop-specific loss data, and the distinction between modeled estimates and verified household-level measurement.
