# Landslide Early Warning And Vulnerability Mapping In The Himalayan Region

## Problem Statement

Rainfall-triggered landslides kill over 4,000 people annually, with South Asia — particularly the Himalayan regions of Nepal, India, and Bangladesh — accounting for over 50 percent of global landslide fatalities. The Himalayan region's extreme topography, monsoon rainfall patterns, seismic activity, and rapid road construction create compounding landslide risk for millions of people living in steep valleys and on unstable slopes. Yet landslide early warning in the Himalayas is almost entirely reactive — warnings are issued after landslides have already occurred, not before.

Satellite-derived slope-stability data from InSAR (interferometric synthetic aperture radar) at 5-20m resolution can detect ground deformation preceding slope failure. Rainfall-trigger models using satellite precipitation data (CHIRPS, GPM) can estimate landslide-triggering rainfall thresholds. Global Landslide Hazard Assessment models map susceptibility at 1km resolution. But linking these datasets to village-level early warning that triggers protective action has not been systematically built for any Himalayan country. The gap between susceptibility mapping and operational early warning is the core challenge.

> Which Himalayan districts have the strongest evidence-backed landslide susceptibility and population exposure, and where are the data gaps most severe for village-level early warning?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: A 2024 study by Petley et al. found that rainfall-triggered landslides cause over 4,000 deaths annually, with South Asia accounting for over 50 percent of global landslide fatalities. Nepal, India, and Bangladesh are the most affected countries.
- Verified fact: InSAR from Sentinel-1 at 5-20m resolution can detect ground deformation preceding slope failure, providing days to weeks of warning potential before catastrophic landslides in suitable terrain.
- Verified fact: Global Landslide Hazard Assessment models (NASA) map rainfall-triggered landslide susceptibility at 1km resolution, but validation in Himalayan terrain is limited — steep slopes, cloud cover, and sparse ground data reduce model accuracy.
- Verified fact: Nepal's Department of Hydrology and Meteorology has a landslide early warning pilot program, but coverage is limited to a few districts and the warning-to-evacuation pipeline has not been tested against actual landslide events.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether InSAR ground-deformation monitoring can provide operationally useful warning lead time in the steep, forested, cloud-covered terrain of the Himalayas where satellite revisit and coherence are limited.
- How rainfall-trigger thresholds derived from global or regional models translate to village-level warning in terrain with extreme micro-climate variation over short distances.
- Whether landslide-susceptibility maps change actual land-use planning, road-construction siting, or village-relocation decisions in Himalayan countries with weak enforcement capacity.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for satellite slope-stability data, rainfall-trigger models, landslide inventory databases, and population-exposure data.
- Sub-national landslide-susceptibility mapping using satellite deformation data and rainfall-trigger models for at least two Himalayan countries.
- Population-exposure analysis linking susceptibility maps to settlement locations and critical infrastructure.
- Red-team review of warning-system overstatement and the risk that susceptibility maps are mistaken for predictions.
- Field-reality review with national disaster-management agencies and community-based preparedness programs in Himalayan districts.

This pack does not accept landslide-prediction claims, village-relocation recommendations, or road-closure decisions based on susceptibility maps alone.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national landslide-susceptibility and population-exposure analysis for at least two Himalayan countries, with explicit documentation of InSAR limitations in steep terrain, rainfall-threshold methodology, and the distinction between susceptibility mapping and operational early warning.
