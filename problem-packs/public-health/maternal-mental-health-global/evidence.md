## Evidence Notes

### WHO Guide for Integration of Perinatal Mental Health 2022

Use this source for the perinatal depression burden baseline — 10-20 percent of women in LMICs experience perinatal depression, with prevalence reaching 25 percent in conflict-affected areas and among adolescents. Detection rates are below 10 percent in most low-income countries, and treatment — whether pharmacological or psychosocial — reaches near zero percent outside research settings.

The prevalence estimates depend heavily on the screening instrument used (EPDS, PHQ-9, SRQ-20), the cutoff score, and whether the screening was validated against a diagnostic interview. Screening tools validated in one population may perform differently in another. Sub-national prevalence estimation requires DHS maternal-mental-health module data, which is currently available in only a limited number of countries.

### Task-Sharing and Thinking Healthy Programme Context

The WHO Thinking Healthy Programme and similar task-sharing approaches using community health workers have demonstrated effectiveness for perinatal depression in multiple LMIC trials (Pakistan, India, South Africa). However, trial efficacy does not guarantee programmatic effectiveness at scale — the gap between what works in a trial and what works in a national program is substantial and under-documented. Any detection-gap analysis that references task-sharing as a solution must explicitly note the scale-up evidence gap.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence accepted only when source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
