# Playbooks

## Analyst Playbook

1. Start with `problem.json`, `evidence.json`, and `tasks.json`.
2. Select one scoped task.
3. Add evidence before adding claims.
4. For dust-exposure mapping: always document AOD retrieval accuracy over bright desert surfaces, AOD-to-ground-PM10 transfer methodology, and boundary-layer height assumptions.
5. For health-impact analysis: always document dust-source toxicology context, confound controls for non-dust PM10 sources, and the distinction between mineral dust mass and biological/chemical contaminant effects.
6. For differential toxicity: always present claims with explicit uncertainty — the finding is not yet consensus and contaminant profiles vary by source region.
7. Mark every operational inference as reviewed, replicated, or blocked.

## Reviewer Playbook

Ask five questions:

1. What claim is being made about dust-storm PM10 exposure or respiratory health impact?
2. What source proves it — and is it satellite AOD, a dust-forecast model, or ground monitoring?
3. What method separates dust-specific PM10 from non-dust sources and controls for confounders?
4. What would make it false — particularly if AOD accuracy over bright surfaces is inadequate, if monitors are too sparse for validation, or if dust-source toxicology varies from the studied source regions?
5. Who could misuse it — particularly to justify land-degradation interventions without health-effectiveness evidence, or to overstate dust-specific health effects when non-dust sources are not controlled?

## Field-Reality Playbook

Do not ask whether a dust-health impact estimate is impressive. Ask whether it would change a named decision for a named user at a useful time, without creating worse incentives. For dust storms in arid LMIC regions, ask specifically:

- Would a Niger Ministry of Environment dust-early-warning officer use this to activate health advisories, or would it be presented as a verified health-impact measurement when it's actually an AOD-proxy estimate with reduced bright-surface accuracy?
- Would a land-degradation program use this to justify reforestation or soil conservation, or would it assume health-effectiveness without direct evidence?
- Would a health ministry use this to pre-position respiratory health resources, or would it ignore the confounding of dust with non-dust PM10 sources?

The answer must include explicit non-use cases before any field-facing output is published.
