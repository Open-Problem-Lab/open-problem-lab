# Youth Skills Training And Employment Outcome Gaps In Low-Income Countries

## Problem Statement

ILO estimates 67 million youth are unemployed globally, with youth unemployment rates 3-4 times adult rates in many low- and middle-income countries. The standard policy response — expanding vocational training and skills programs — has produced mixed results. Systematic reviews of youth employment interventions find that training programs increase employment rates by 2-5 percentage points on average, but effects vary enormously by program design, local labor-market conditions, and whether training is linked to actual employer demand. The gap between training enrollment and verified employment outcomes is the core evaluation problem.

Sub-national data on where training programs exist, what skills they teach, who enrolls, and whether graduates find employment is almost entirely absent from public datasets in most LMICs. Training-provider registries are fragmented across government ministries, NGOs, and private operators. Employment-outcome tracking — the critical effectiveness indicator — is conducted by fewer than 10 percent of training programs in most LMIC contexts. Without verified outcome data, skills-training investment cannot be evidence-targeted.

> Which sub-national areas in LMICs have the strongest evidence-backed gap between youth skills-training availability and verified employment outcomes, and where is the data most insufficient?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: ILO's 2024 Global Employment Trends for Youth estimates 67 million unemployed youth globally, with youth-to-adult unemployment ratios of 3-4x in many LMICs and skills mismatch identified as a primary barrier to youth employment.
- Verified fact: Systematic reviews of youth employment interventions find that vocational training programs increase employment rates by 2-5 percentage points on average, but effects are highly heterogeneous — programs linked to employer demand show 8-12 percentage point gains while standalone programs show near-zero effects.
- Verified fact: Training-provider registries are fragmented across government ministries, NGOs, and private operators in most LMICs — no single dataset captures the full landscape of skills-training availability at sub-national scale.
- Verified fact: Employment-outcome tracking is conducted by fewer than 10 percent of training programs in most LMIC contexts, making it impossible to systematically assess which programs actually improve employment outcomes.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether the 2-5 percentage point average employment effect from training programs generalizes across LMIC labor-market contexts, or whether effects are concentrated in specific sectors and regions.
- How informal-sector employment and self-employment interact with formal training outcomes — many training graduates enter informal work that is not captured in employment statistics.
- Whether skills-gap maps change training-provider targeting, curriculum design, or employer-engagement decisions.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for training-provider data, employment-outcome surveys, and labor-market information systems.
- Sub-national training-availability mapping using provider registries and program documentation.
- Employment-outcome analysis linking training data to labor-force survey and enterprise survey data.
- Red-team review of training-effectiveness overstatement and the risk of expanding programs without outcome evidence.
- Field-reality review with training providers, employer organizations, and labor-market information systems.

This pack does not accept training-program design recommendations, curriculum proposals, or unvalidated claims that specific training programs improve employment outcomes.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national skills-training availability and employment-outcome analysis for at least two LMICs, with explicit documentation of training-provider data fragmentation, employment-outcome tracking limitations, and the distinction between training enrollment and verified employment impact.
