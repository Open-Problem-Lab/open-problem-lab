# Playbooks

## Analyst Playbook

1. Start with `problem.json`, `evidence.json`, and `tasks.json`.
2. Select one scoped task.
3. Add evidence before adding claims.
4. For financing-gap mapping: always document whether data is national aggregate or sub-national — national statistics mask enormous district-level financing gaps. Document where sub-national data is absent.
5. For financing-structure analysis: always document the missing-middle barrier — project sizes ($50K-$5M) too large for microfinance, too small for commercial project finance. Document currency risk, transaction costs, and perceived-risk barriers.
6. For volume-vs-access distinction: always document that increasing finance volume alone does not close access gaps — structure, instruments, and intermediation all matter for reaching last-mile populations.
7. Mark every operational inference as reviewed, replicated, or blocked.

## Reviewer Playbook

Ask five questions:

1. What claim is being made about clean energy financing gaps?
2. What source proves it — and is it IRENA aggregate data, Bloomberg NEF project data, or CPI concessional-finance analysis?
3. What method translates national investment statistics into sub-national gap claims, and what is the sub-national data availability?
4. What would make it false — particularly if national aggregates mask sub-national targeting needs, if the missing-middle barrier is structural rather than volume-based, or if concessional-finance allocation data is too coarse for targeting?
5. Who could misuse it — particularly to claim that increasing finance volume alone closes access gaps, to overgeneralize national statistics to sub-national contexts, or to ignore missing-middle structural barriers?

## Field-Reality Playbook

Do not ask whether a financing-gap map is impressive. Ask whether it would change a named decision for a named user at a useful time, without creating worse incentives. For clean energy financing in low-income countries, ask specifically:

- Would an IFC distributed energy financing officer use this to target concessional-finance allocation to last-mile projects, or would aggregate statistics mask the actual sub-national targeting needs?
- Would a distributed energy enterprise use this to identify underserved markets, or would volume-gap claims imply that more finance alone solves the missing-middle problem?
- Would a concessional-finance manager use this to design de-risking instruments, or would it ignore that structural barriers (currency risk, transaction costs) require instrument innovation, not just more capital?

The answer must include explicit non-use cases before any field-facing output is published.
