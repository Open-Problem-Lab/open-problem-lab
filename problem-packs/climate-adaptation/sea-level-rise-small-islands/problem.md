# Sea-Level Rise Coastal Exposure And Adaptation Prioritization In Small Island Developing States

## Problem Statement

Small Island Developing States are the canary in the climate coal mine. With maximum elevations often under 5 meters and populations concentrated centimeters above sea level on narrow coastal strips, sea-level rise is not a future concern — it is a present threat to habitability. General circulation models project global mean sea-level rise, but projecting what happens at a specific atoll or coastal village requires downscaling that compounds uncertainty. The hardest problem is not projecting SLR — it is deciding what to do with uncertain projections when the cost of inaction is displacement and the cost of maladaptation is wasted resources that SIDS cannot afford.

> Which island-scale sea-level rise, coastal erosion, and storm-surge compound exposure signals are evidence-backed enough to support adaptation prioritization without overstating projection precision?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: The IPCC AR6 projects global mean sea-level rise of 0.4-0.8 meters by 2100 under intermediate scenarios and up to 1.0-2.0 meters under high-emission scenarios, with committed multi-meter rise over centuries.
- Verified fact: Many Small Island Developing States — including Kiribati, Tuvalu, Maldives, and Marshall Islands — have maximum elevations under 5 meters and substantial populations and infrastructure within 100 meters of the shoreline.
- Verified fact: A 2021 Nature Climate Change study found that sea-level rise is already increasing the frequency of coastal flooding in tropical regions and that what were once 100-year flood events are occurring every few years in some atoll nations.
- Verified fact: Coastal erosion and saltwater intrusion into freshwater lenses are compounding threats that interact with SLR but are harder to model at island scale.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether global SLR projections can be meaningfully downscaled to individual islands given local subsidence, tectonics, and ocean-circulation effects.
- How storm-surge and wave-runup modeling at atoll scale compares against observed flood extents.
- Whether adaptation prioritization frameworks that work for larger nations are appropriate for SIDS with limited fiscal space and governance capacity.
- How managed retreat and international migration frameworks interact with adaptation investment timing.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source quality assessment.
- Dataset availability including SLR projections, coastal elevation data, population data, and storm-surge models.
- Island-scale coastal exposure mapping with uncertainty communication.
- Compound-risk analysis combining SLR, storm surge, and coastal erosion.
- Red-team review of over-precise projections and maladaptation risk.
- Field-reality review with SIDS adaptation planning context.

This pack does not accept relocation recommendations, international migration policy, adaptation investment allocation recommendations, or unvalidated local SLR projections presented as definitive.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated island-scale coastal exposure analysis for at least two SIDS, with explicit SLR projection uncertainty and compound-risk documentation, that a domain reviewer and field-reality reviewer judge suitable for adaptation planning discussion rather than investment decisions.
