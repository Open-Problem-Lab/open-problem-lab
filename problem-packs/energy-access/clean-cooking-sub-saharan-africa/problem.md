# Clean Cooking Adoption Barriers And Health Impact In Sub-Saharan Africa

## Problem Statement

Household air pollution from cooking with solid fuels kills over 3 million people per year — more than malaria, tuberculosis, and HIV/AIDS combined. Sub-Saharan Africa has the lowest access to clean cooking of any region, with over 80 percent of households burning wood, charcoal, or dung indoors. The problem is well understood in aggregate. What is underdeveloped is the verified spatial knowledge of where the health burden is highest and which adoption barriers are most tractable where.

> Which sub-national household air pollution burden and clean cooking adoption barrier signals are evidence-backed enough to support intervention prioritization?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: WHO reports 3.2 million premature deaths annually from household air pollution, with Sub-Saharan Africa carrying a disproportionate share due to low clean cooking access.
- Verified fact: The IEA's Africa Energy Outlook documents that over 900 million people in Sub-Saharan Africa lack access to clean cooking, with the number increasing despite some national programs due to population growth outpacing adoption.
- Verified fact: A 2021 Lancet Planetary Health study estimated that transitioning 50 percent of solid fuel users to LPG or electricity in Sub-Saharan Africa could avert over 400,000 deaths annually, but cost, infrastructure, and cultural barriers block adoption.
- Verified fact: The Clean Cooking Alliance catalogs over 300 clean cooking enterprises across Africa, but most operate at small scale with limited verified health impact data.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether sub-national household air pollution exposure data is accurate enough for intervention targeting.
- How fuel stacking behavior — where households use both clean and solid fuels — affects modeled health benefits.
- Whether cost-barrier data at sub-national scale is reliable enough to prioritize subsidy allocation.
- How cultural preferences and cooking practices interact with technology adoption across different regions.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source quality assessment.
- Dataset availability including household surveys, health data, and clean cooking enterprise data.
- Sub-national health burden estimation from household air pollution.
- Adoption barrier classification and spatial analysis.
- Red-team review of overestimated health benefits.
- Field-reality review with clean cooking program implementation context.

This pack does not accept specific clean cooking technology recommendations, subsidy allocation proposals, or unvalidated health-benefit forecasts.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national analysis linking household air pollution burden to clean cooking adoption barriers, with explicit uncertainty, that a domain reviewer judges suitable for intervention discussion rather than allocation decisions.
