## Evidence Notes

### FAO Grassland Degradation 2023

Use this source for the global grassland degradation baseline and Central Asian context. The 20-30 percent figure is a global estimate — degradation rates vary by methodology and country. Do not use this as proof of degradation in any specific Central Asian sub-national area without satellite or field verification. The FAO assessment does not provide sub-national maps suitable for pastoral livelihood analysis.

### MODIS NDVI Grassland Monitoring

Use MODIS NDVI as the primary vegetation-productivity indicator for grassland condition trends. The 250m resolution may miss paddock-scale heterogeneity around wells and settlements. The critical analytical challenge is disambiguating climate-driven productivity fluctuations (which may recover in wet years) from persistent degradation (which does not). This requires multi-decade time series and statistical trend methods — short-term NDVI declines during drought years do not prove degradation. The Rangeland Analysis Platform at 30m has limited Central Asian validation compared to North America.

### Mongolia Dzud Livestock Mortality

Use this source for understanding extreme weather event impacts on pastoral livelihoods. Dzud events are a major confound for grassland degradation analysis — livestock mortality spikes during dzud years may reflect extreme weather rather than persistent land degradation. The relationship between degradation and dzud vulnerability is complex: degraded pastures reduce livestock body condition pre-dzud, but dzud severity depends primarily on winter weather conditions. Mongolia data is substantially better than other Central Asian countries — do not extrapolate Mongolia findings to Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, or Kyrgyzstan without documenting the data gap.

### Pastoral Data Sparsity Documentation

Use this source to document the fundamental evidence constraint for this pack: Central Asian pastoralist populations are among the most data-sparse in the world. Livestock census data is irregular, household livelihood surveys are infrequent, and pastoralist mobility makes conventional sampling difficult. Any grassland degradation-to-livelihood analysis for Central Asia must explicitly document this data sparsity and present results as proxy-based risk indicators, not verified livelihood impact measurements.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack.
