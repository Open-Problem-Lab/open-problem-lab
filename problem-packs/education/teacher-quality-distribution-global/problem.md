# Teacher Qualification Distribution And Deployment Equity Gaps In Low-Income Countries

## Problem Statement

UNESCO estimates a global shortage of 44 million teachers to achieve universal primary and secondary education by 2030, with the most severe shortages in Sub-Saharan Africa where pupil-to-trained-teacher ratios exceed 200:1 in some districts. But the shortage headline masks a deeper equity problem: within countries, qualified teachers concentrate in urban areas and wealthier districts, leaving rural and low-income communities with unqualified, absent, or temporary teachers. The deployment gap — where qualified teachers are versus where they are most needed — is the core equity challenge.

Teacher-quality data comes from national education management information systems (EMIS) and Service Delivery Indicator surveys, but coverage varies enormously across countries. Sub-national teacher-distribution data exists for some countries through EMIS but is rarely linked to learning-outcome data at the same geographic grain. The absence of systematic teacher-quality-to-learning-outcome linkage means that deployment-equity interventions cannot be evidence-targeted.

> Which sub-national districts in low-income countries have the strongest evidence-backed gap between teacher qualification levels and learning outcomes, and where is the data most insufficient for deployment-equity analysis?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: UNESCO's 2024 Global Report on Teachers estimates a global shortage of 44 million teachers for universal primary and secondary education by 2030, with Sub-Saharan Africa facing the most severe shortages and pupil-to-trained-teacher ratios exceeding 200:1 in some districts.
- Verified fact: Service Delivery Indicator surveys in multiple African countries document that teacher-content knowledge and pedagogical-skill scores vary dramatically within countries, with rural schools scoring 20-40 percent lower than urban schools on standardized teacher assessments.
- Verified fact: Teacher absenteeism rates of 15-25 percent are documented in multiple low-income countries, meaning that even when qualified teachers are deployed to rural areas, effective teaching time is substantially reduced.
- Verified fact: National EMIS data provides school-level teacher counts and qualification data for some countries, but data quality, update frequency, and public accessibility vary enormously — many countries do not publish sub-national teacher-distribution data.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether EMIS teacher-qualification data accurately reflects classroom-level teaching quality, or whether qualification is a weak proxy for actual pedagogical effectiveness.
- How teacher-deployment policies (rural incentives, rotation requirements, hardship allowances) interact with qualification distribution in practice.
- Whether deployment-equity maps change teacher-transfer policies, rural-incentive program design, or training-institute placement decisions.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for teacher-quality data (EMIS, SDI surveys), learning-outcome data, and deployment-policy documentation.
- Sub-national teacher-qualification distribution mapping using EMIS and SDI data.
- Teacher-quality-to-learning-outcome linkage analysis at district scale with appropriate confound controls.
- Deployment-equity analysis identifying districts with the largest qualification-to-need gaps.
- Red-team review of qualification-proxy overstatement and the risk that deployment maps are used for punitive transfers.
- Field-reality review with education ministries and teacher-training institutions.

This pack does not accept teacher-transfer recommendations, rural-incentive program designs, or unvalidated claims that teacher qualification directly causes learning-outcome differences.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national teacher-deployment equity analysis for at least two low-income countries, with explicit documentation of EMIS data quality, qualification-proxy limitations, and the distinction between teacher presence and teaching effectiveness.
