# Sickle Cell Disease Newborn Screening And Treatment Access In Sub-Saharan Africa

## Problem Statement

300,000 babies are born with sickle cell disease annually — 75 percent in Sub-Saharan Africa. Without newborn screening and penicillin prophylaxis, 50-90 percent die before turning five. Yet fewer than 10 percent of SCD births are identified through screening, and hydroxyurea reaches a fraction of those who need it. Sub-national sickle cell prevalence estimates exist from DHS sickle cell testing modules and published micro-mapping studies. But linking prevalence geography to screening and treatment coverage has not been done systematically.

> Which sub-national areas in Sub-Saharan Africa have the highest evidence-backed gap between SCD birth prevalence and newborn screening and treatment coverage?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: WHO estimates 300,000+ babies born with SCD annually, 75 percent in Sub-Saharan Africa, with 50-90 percent mortality before age five without care.
- Verified fact: DHS surveys have included sickle cell testing in several SSA countries, providing sub-national prevalence estimates for the first time at scale.
- Verified fact: Newborn screening coverage for SCD is under 10 percent across Sub-Saharan Africa, concentrated in capital-city hospitals, leaving the vast majority of affected births undetected.
- Verified fact: Penicillin prophylaxis and hydroxyurea can reduce SCD mortality by over 70 percent when initiated early, but treatment access is extremely limited outside a few specialized centers.

## Uncertain Areas

Whether DHS sickle cell testing has sufficient spatial coverage for district-level birth-prevalence estimation. Whether screening and treatment facility data is accessible and current. Whether gap maps change SCD program investment.

## Initial Scope

Evidence inventory, SCD prevalence estimation, screening-coverage mapping, treatment-gap analysis, red-team review, field-reality review.

## Done Condition For V0

One reviewed and replicated sub-national SCD treatment gap analysis for at least two SSA countries.
