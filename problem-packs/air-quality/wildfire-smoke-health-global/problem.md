# Wildfire Smoke PM2.5 And Respiratory Health In Fire-Prone Regions

## Problem Statement

Wildfire smoke now contributes up to 25 percent of global ambient PM2.5, and the fraction is rising as climate change extends fire seasons across boreal, temperate, and tropical regions. Smoke-derived PM2.5 appears to be 2-3 times more toxic per unit mass than other PM2.5 sources — possibly due to its oxidative-potential and co-pollutant profile — though this differential toxicity finding requires further replication. NOAA's Hazard Mapping System has produced daily satellite-derived smoke-plume polygons since 2006, and GEOS-Chem provides composition-resolved PM2.5 estimates. But these exposure datasets have not been systematically validated against ground-level PM2.5 monitors and linked to respiratory health outcomes at sub-national scale in the fire-prone regions where the burden is highest.

> Which fire-prone regions have the strongest evidence-backed relationship between wildfire-smoke-specific PM2.5 exposure and respiratory health outcomes, after accounting for non-smoke PM2.5 confounders?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: A 2021 Lancet Planetary Health study by Burke et al. estimated that wildfire smoke contributes up to 25 percent of global ambient PM2.5, with long-term smoke exposure associated with all-cause mortality and smoke PM2.5 potentially 2-3 times more toxic per unit than other PM2.5 components.
- Verified fact: NOAA Hazard Mapping System provides daily satellite-derived smoke-plume polygons at approximately 1 km resolution going back to 2006, classifying smoke density into light, medium, and heavy categories from GOES and polar-orbiting satellite imagery.
- Verified fact: GEOS-Chem global chemical-transport model provides composition-resolved PM2.5 estimates including smoke-specific black carbon and organic carbon fractions at approximately 0.25 degree resolution.
- Verified fact: Multiple studies have found 5-15 percent increases in respiratory emergency department visits and 1-3 percent increases in all-cause mortality on wildfire smoke days, but the studies are concentrated in North America, Australia, and Southern Europe with limited LMIC evidence.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether NOAA HMS smoke-plume polygons correlate with ground-level PM2.5 measurements strongly enough to serve as a validated exposure proxy, particularly in Southeast Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa where ground monitors are sparse.
- The extent to which smoke-specific PM2.5 differential toxicity is a consistent biological phenomenon versus a statistical artifact of confounding by socioeconomic vulnerability and housing quality.
- Whether respiratory health-outcome data from health management information systems in LMICs has sufficient spatial and temporal resolution for smoke-exposure epidemiology.
- How wildfire-smoke health-impact estimates should be communicated without causing panic during active fire events or undermining prescribed-burning programs that reduce catastrophic fire risk.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for satellite smoke-detection, chemical-transport models, ground PM2.5 monitors, and respiratory health-utilization data.
- Smoke-exposure estimation combining NOAA HMS plume data, GEOS-Chem composition, and ground-monitor validation.
- Epidemiological analysis linking smoke-specific PM2.5 exposure to respiratory health outcomes with appropriate confound controls.
- Red-team review of exposure-misclassification risks and health-impact overstatement.
- Field-reality review with air-quality management agencies and health-ministry environmental health units.

This pack does not accept fire-management recommendations, prescribed-burning policy, or unvalidated health-impact forecasts for individual fire events.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated smoke-exposure health-impact analysis for at least two fire-prone regions, with explicit documentation of satellite-to-ground exposure validation, confounding-structure justification, and non-smoke PM2.5 adjustment methodology.
