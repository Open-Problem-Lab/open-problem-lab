# Clean Energy Financing Access Gaps In Low-Income Countries

## Problem Statement

IRENA estimates that clean energy investment in low-income countries needs to increase 7-fold to meet SDG7 and climate goals, with financing gaps largest for distributed renewables — mini-grids, solar home systems, and clean cooking solutions that serve the last-mile populations most in need. The financing gap is not just about total investment volume — it is about the structure of available finance. Concessional finance flows disproportionately to utility-scale projects in middle-income countries, while the distributed energy enterprises serving rural populations in low-income countries face a "missing middle" — too large for microfinance, too small for commercial project finance, and too risky for most private investors.

Investment-flow data from IRENA, Bloomberg NEF, and the Climate Policy Initiative tracks global and national clean energy finance, but sub-national data on where financing reaches — and where it does not — is almost entirely absent. Project-pipeline databases document funded projects but not the pipeline of viable projects that cannot access finance. The gap between financing need and financing access at sub-national scale is the core measurement problem.

> Which sub-national areas in low-income countries have the strongest evidence-backed clean energy financing gaps, and what financing-structure barriers are most tractable?

## Known Facts

- Verified fact: IRENA's 2024 World Energy Transitions Outlook estimates clean energy investment in low-income countries needs to increase 7-fold to meet SDG7 and climate goals, with distributed renewables facing the largest financing gaps.
- Verified fact: The "missing middle" financing gap is well-documented — distributed energy enterprises serving rural populations face project sizes ($50K-$5M) too large for microfinance but too small for commercial project finance, with perceived risk profiles that deter most private investors.
- Verified fact: Concessional climate finance flows disproportionately to utility-scale projects in middle-income countries — the least-developed countries receive a fraction of per-capita clean energy finance relative to their access gaps.
- Verified fact: Bloomberg NEF and Climate Policy Initiative track global and national clean energy investment flows, but sub-national data on where financing reaches is almost entirely absent — national aggregates mask enormous district-level gaps.

## Uncertain Areas

- Whether sub-national financing-gap data exists at sufficient detail for targeting analysis, or whether only national aggregate statistics are available in most countries.
- How financing-structure barriers (missing middle, currency risk, transaction costs) interact with energy-access gaps in determining where clean energy deployment actually occurs.
- Whether financing-gap maps change concessional-finance allocation, enterprise-support program design, or de-risking instrument deployment.

## Initial Scope

This pack accepts work on:

- Evidence inventory and source-quality assessment for investment-flow data, project-pipeline databases, and financing-structure analysis.
- Sub-national financing-gap mapping using investment-flow data and energy-access gap data.
- Financing-structure analysis documenting missing-middle barriers, concessional-finance allocation, and de-risking instrument coverage.
- Red-team review of financing-gap overstatement and the risk that aggregate statistics mask sub-national targeting needs.
- Field-reality review with development-finance institutions, energy-access enterprises, and concessional-finance managers.

This pack does not accept specific investment recommendations, de-risking instrument designs, or unvalidated claims that increasing finance volume alone closes energy-access gaps.

## Done Condition For V0

The pack is useful when it can produce one reviewed and replicated sub-national clean energy financing gap analysis for at least two low-income countries, with explicit documentation of investment-flow data limitations, financing-structure barriers, and the distinction between financing volume and financing access.
