# Evidence Ledger

## Current Evidence Records

The machine-readable ledger is `evidence.json`.

## Evidence Notes

### Andersson et al. 2012 Global Iodine Status

Use this source for the most recent globally comprehensive iodine status estimate: 2 billion people with insufficient intake, 241 million school-age children. This is the foundational burden figure. Do not treat it as current; it is based on 2011 data and no update has been published since.

### WHO/UNICEF Iodine Fact Sheet (2024)

Use this source for WHO's official framing of iodine deficiency as the most prevalent preventable cause of brain damage and the recommendation for universal salt iodization. Do not use it for sub-national status data; it provides global-level framing only.

### IGN Global Scorecard (2024)

Use this source for the most comprehensive country-by-country iodine status and salt iodization coverage data available. Coverage definitions vary across countries; the scorecard is the best available but not standardized. Use it to identify coverage gaps, not to make precise cross-country comparisons without checking the underlying survey methodology.

### UNICEF SOWC 2019

Use this source for regional iodine burden estimates and UNICEF's programmatic framing. The 86 percent household coverage figure refers to regions with iodization programs, not globally. Do not use it for country-specific or sub-national estimates.

### Pearce et al. 2016 Pregnancy Iodine

Use this source for evidence that iodine requirements increase by approximately 50 percent during pregnancy and that mild deficiency has neurodevelopmental consequences. Do not use it for population-level iodine status; it is a clinical review. The causal evidence for mild deficiency effects is debated due to confounding.

### Copenhagen Consensus 2008

Use this source for the cost-effectiveness framing of salt iodization at approximately 0.05 USD per child per year. The estimate is from 2008 and may be outdated. Do not treat the 30:1 benefit-cost ratio as a current figure; it depends on assumptions about productivity impact and discount rates.

### WHO/UNICEF/ICCIDD Salt Iodization Guideline (2014)

Use this source for the international standard on iodization levels (20-40 mg/kg) and the recommendation for mandatory universal salt iodization. Do not assume the guideline addresses processed-food salt, which has emerged as a major gap since 2014.

### IGN Backsliding Reports (2023)

Use this source for evidence that coverage reversals are occurring in specific countries. Treat the coverage figures as approximate and check whether more recent national survey data is available. The IGN is both an advocate and a technical body; the data is grounded in surveys but the framing may emphasize reversals.

### Dzeuta et al. 2022 Small-Scale Salt Producers

Use this source for evidence that small-scale salt producers in Sub-Saharan Africa operate outside formal iodization supply chains and that iodine loss during storage and cooking may cause coverage estimates to overstate actual iodine intake. Do not generalize beyond the studied countries without noting the limitation.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack. Iodine status estimates must specify the UIC cutoff applied, population group surveyed, survey year, and geographic grain. Salt iodization coverage estimates must specify the testing method, adequately iodized threshold, and whether coverage is measured at household, retail, or production level.
