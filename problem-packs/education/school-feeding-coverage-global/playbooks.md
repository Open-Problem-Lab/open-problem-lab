# Playbooks

## Analyst Playbook

1. Start with `problem.json`, `evidence.json`, and `tasks.json`.
2. Select one scoped task.
3. Add evidence before adding claims.
4. For coverage mapping: always document whether data is national aggregate or sub-national — national statistics mask enormous district-level gaps. Document where sub-national data is absent.
5. For nutritional-impact analysis: always document meal-quality variation — a coverage metric without nutritional-content data does not demonstrate nutritional improvement. Distinguish enrollment effects (strong evidence) from nutrition effects (weaker evidence).
6. For cost-effectiveness: always document which outcome measure is used for comparison — school feeding performs differently on enrollment vs. nutrition vs. learning outcomes.
7. Mark every operational inference as reviewed, replicated, or blocked.

## Reviewer Playbook

Ask five questions:

1. What claim is being made about school feeding coverage or nutritional impact?
2. What source proves it — and is it WFP national survey data, sub-national program data, or nutrition-outcome data?
3. What method distinguishes program existence from verified nutritional impact, and what is the meal-quality documentation?
4. What would make it false — particularly if national statistics mask district-level gaps, if meal quality is inadequate, if enrollment effects are conflated with nutrition effects, or if concurrent nutrition interventions confound the analysis?
5. Who could misuse it — particularly to claim nutritional impact without meal-quality data, to expand programs without outcome evidence, or to use national statistics that mask district-level gaps?

## Field-Reality Playbook

Do not ask whether a coverage map is impressive. Ask whether it would change a named decision for a named user at a useful time, without creating worse incentives. For school feeding in low-income countries, ask specifically:

- Would a WFP Ethiopia school feeding coordinator use this to target program expansion to uncovered districts, or would national coverage statistics mask the actual district-level gaps?
- Would an education ministry use this to justify feeding-program investment, or would it treat coverage as nutritional impact without meal-quality evidence?
- Would a nutrition funder use this to compare school feeding with alternative interventions, or would it ignore that enrollment effects (strong) and nutrition effects (weak) must be distinguished?

The answer must include explicit non-use cases before any field-facing output is published.
