# Playbooks

## Analyst Playbook

1. Start with `problem.json`, `evidence.json`, and `tasks.json`.
2. Select one scoped task.
3. Add evidence before adding claims.
4. For susceptibility mapping: always document InSAR limitations in steep, forested, cloud-covered Himalayan terrain — coherence loss, geometric distortions, and atmospheric phase delays.
5. For rainfall-threshold analysis: always document that satellite precipitation has reduced accuracy in mountainous terrain with complex orography — micro-climate variation over short distances means regional thresholds may not apply to specific villages.
6. For susceptibility claims: always present results as probabilistic relative likelihood, not as deterministic predictions — susceptibility maps do not tell you when or where a specific landslide will occur.
7. Mark every operational inference as reviewed, replicated, or blocked.

## Reviewer Playbook

Ask five questions:

1. What claim is being made about landslide susceptibility or early warning capability?
2. What source proves it — and is it satellite InSAR, a rainfall-trigger model, or a landslide inventory?
3. What method translates satellite data into susceptibility estimates, and what are the terrain-specific limitations?
4. What would make it false — particularly if InSAR can't detect deformation in forested terrain, if rainfall thresholds don't capture micro-climate variation, or if the susceptibility map is mistaken for a prediction?
5. Who could misuse it — particularly to make village-relocation decisions, road-closure decisions, or claims about when and where specific landslides will occur?

## Field-Reality Playbook

Do not ask whether a susceptibility map is impressive. Ask whether it would change a named decision for a named user at a useful time, without creating worse incentives. For Himalayan landslide early warning, ask specifically:

- Would a Nepal NDRRMA officer use this to prioritize district-level early warning pilot expansion, or would the susceptibility map be treated as a prediction that specific villages will be hit?
- Would a road-construction authority use this to avoid high-susceptibility routes, or would it ignore the probabilistic nature of the map and make deterministic routing decisions?
- Would a community-based preparedness program use this to target outreach, or would it create false confidence in monitoring capability that doesn't exist at village level?

The answer must include explicit non-use cases before any field-facing output is published.
