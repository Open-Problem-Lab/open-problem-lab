## Evidence Notes

### Wesseling et al. 2020 (Mesoamerica)

Use this source for the best available prevalence estimates of CKDu among agricultural workers in Mesoamerica and for the systematic assessment that heat-stress patterns are consistent with the geographic distribution but do not exclude agrochemical co-factors. Do not use it as proof that heat-stress is the sole cause.

### Jayatilake et al. 2015 (Sri Lanka)

Use this source for community-level prevalence in Sri Lanka's North Central Province and for the demographic profile (young males, no conventional risk factors). Do not use it as evidence for or against any specific causal hypothesis; the study measured exposure through self-report only.

### PAHO El Salvador CKD Report (2020)

Use this source for the mortality burden framing: El Salvador's CKD hospital death rate is the highest in the Americas. Do not use it for prevalence or incidence estimates, as it reports mortality only and aggregates all CKD.

### Glaser et al. 2016 (Heat-Stress Hypothesis)

Use this source for the mechanistic argument linking heat stress, dehydration, hyperuricemia, and kidney injury. This is a hypothesis paper, not a data study. Do not use it as proof that heat-stress is the primary cause; it proposes a mechanism and cites supporting indirect evidence.

### Jayasumana et al. 2015 (Glyphosate Hypothesis)

Use this source for the agrochemical hypothesis that glyphosate-metal complexes in hard water contribute to CKDu in Sri Lanka. The evidence is ecological and the mechanism has not been independently replicated. Do not use it as proof that pesticides cause CKDu, and note the debated funding context.

### Roncal Jimenez et al. 2016 (Biomarker Study)

Use this source for the strongest direct evidence that heat exposure in sugar cane workers produces acute kidney injury biomarkers consistent with the heat-stress pathway. Do not use it as proof of progression to chronic CKD; the study covers one harvest season and does not measure agrochemical exposure.

## Evidence Quality Rule

Evidence is not accepted because it sounds plausible. It is accepted when the source, method, limitations, and confidence are explicit enough for a reviewer to attack. CKDu evidence must specify study design (cross-sectional, cohort, experimental), diagnostic criteria (eGFR threshold, albuminuria, kidney injury biomarkers), exposure assessment method (self-report, measurement, biomarker), and whether the study can distinguish heat-stress from agrochemical mechanisms. A study that cannot distinguish the two must say so.
