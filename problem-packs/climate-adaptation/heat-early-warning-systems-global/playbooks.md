# Playbooks

## Analyst Playbook

1. Start with `problem.json`, `evidence.json`, and `tasks.json`.
2. Select one scoped task.
3. Add evidence before adding claims.
4. For warning-system coverage mapping: always document the distinction between nominal system existence and verified operational capacity — a WMO listing does not mean warnings reach vulnerable populations or trigger protective action.
5. For effectiveness evaluation: always document mortality-data quality, climate-trend adjustment methodology, and confound controls for secular trends in healthcare, housing, and air conditioning.
6. For LMIC-transferability: always document whether effectiveness estimates are from the analyzed LMIC context or transferred from high-income country studies with explicit transferability caveats.
7. Mark every operational inference as reviewed, replicated, or blocked.

## Reviewer Playbook

Ask five questions:

1. What claim is being made about heat early warning system coverage or mortality-reduction effectiveness?
2. What source proves it — and is it a WMO existence indicator, a mortality-trend analysis, or a single-city case study?
3. What method separates warning-system effects from secular trends in healthcare, housing, and air conditioning?
4. What would make it false — particularly if mortality data is inadequate for trend analysis, if climate trends shift baselines, or if the Ahmedabad case doesn't transfer to other LMIC contexts?
5. Who could misuse it — particularly to claim heat mortality is solved in a country with a listed but non-operational warning system, or to justify underinvestment in other climate-adaptation measures?

## Field-Reality Playbook

Do not ask whether a coverage-effectiveness analysis is impressive. Ask whether it would change a named decision for a named user at a useful time, without creating worse incentives. For heat early warning, ask specifically:

- Would an Indian Meteorological Department heat-wave coordinator use this to justify expanded Heat Action Plan funding, or would it be used to claim the problem is solved when effectiveness is unverified?
- Would a climate-adaptation funder use this to prioritize heat warning over other investments, or would it overstate effectiveness based on limited LMIC evidence?
- Would a disaster-management authority use this to identify coverage gaps, or would it imply protection exists in areas where listed systems are non-operational?

The answer must include explicit non-use cases before any field-facing output is published.
